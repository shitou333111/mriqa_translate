Biological Effects of RF Fields

How do radiofrequency fields affect biological tissues?  
Radiofrequency (RF) fields are part of the electromagnetic spectrum used for communication and spectroscopy, commonly defined as spanning frequencies from approximately 3 kHz to 300 GHz. According to Maxwell’s equations, a time-varying magnetic field is necessarily accompanied by a time-varying electric field, and vice versa. Consequently, RF irradiation in MRI always consists of coupled magnetic (B) and electric (E) field components.
These fields interact not only with nuclear spins, but also with electrons, ions, atoms, and entire molecules. As a result, RF exposure can produce a wide range of physical and biological effects. These effects are generally classified as either non-thermal or thermal in origin.
Non-Thermal Effects
Considerable controversy​ exists concerning whether non-ionizing irradiation in the RF spectrum causes genetic, cellular, or physiologic damage unrelated to tissue heating. Theoretical mechanisms of non-thermal injury include generation of reactive oxygen species ("oxidative stress") and alteration of calcium flux across cell membranes. Most research into this topic has focused on long-term or high intensity exposures, often at frequencies higher or lower than those used in MRI. Even strong critics of current safety standards generally agree that non-thermal effects, even if they exist, are unlikely to be significant in the MRI setting. These issues are discussed in more detail with extensive references in another Q&A.
Thermal Effects
By contrast, thermal effects are universally recognized to produce a large number of potential biological changes, including impaired thermoregulation, alteration of chemical reaction rates, pain, and burns. ​ Although most readers are probably aware that these effects exist, they may be surprised to learn the following:
  1. Only a tiny fraction (typically < 2%) of transmitted RF power is absorbed by nuclear spins.
  2. The nuclear energy absorbed and subsequently transferred to the lattice via T1​ relaxation makes a negligible contribution to tissue heating.
  3. The accompanying electric field (E)—rather than the magnetic excitation field (B1)—is responsible for nearly all RF-induced thermal effects in MRI.
More about the Electric Field (E)
The time-dependent E and B1 fields are related by the Faraday-Maxwell equation which can be written in differential form as: ∇ ✕ E = −∂B1/∂t. The left hand side of this equation contains a vector operator called "the curl" (∇ ✕), which characterizes the local circulation of the electric field, while the right hand side is a "derivative" (∂ /∂t), representing the time rate of change of B1. This equation shows that a time-varying magnetic field necessarily generates a circulating electric field. The electric (E) field can thus be visualized as "curling around" the magnetic (B1) field in concentric loops that encircle regions where B1 is changing in time, with the magnitude and orientation of these loops determined by the instantaneous strength, direction, and time variation of B1. Both fields oscillate at the same frequency (ωo = 2​ π fo), so the induced electric field reverses direction every half-cycle, alternating between clockwise and counterclockwise circulation.
Relation of magnetic (B1) and electric (E) fields as defined by the Faraday-Maxwell equation. The lines of the E field "curl" around the B1 field. The direction of E is defined as the direction a hypothetical positive charge would move (or an electric current would flow) at a given point in space and time.
The E field deposits energy into tissues by two basic mechanisms: 1) resistive heating and 2) dielectric heating.
Resistive Heating
Pure water is a poor conductor, but biological tissues, which contain water plus dissolved electrolytes, conduct currents fairly well. The charge carriers in tissue are principally Na+, K+, Cl−, and HCO3− ions. ​Voltage gradients in the electric field accelerate these ions, imparting them with kinetic energy and creating an induced current. When these charge carriers undergo inelastic collisions with other ions, atoms, or molecules, their kinetic energy is transferred into random molecular motion, producing thermal energy (heat). This process is analogous to resistive loss of energy in a conventional electrical circuit governed by Ohm's Law. It is therefore alternatively referred to as Joule, Resistive, or Ohmic heating. 
Dielectric Heating
The water molecule is polar due to its electronegative O atom
Many molecules in the body are polar, especially water, based on its highly electronegative oxygen component. Polar molecules tend to align with an externally applied electric field. When the field changes, so do their orientations. In the rapidly oscillating RF electric fields present in MRI, these rotating dipoles interact and collide with other molecules, converting their ordered motion into random molecular motion and thus generating heat.. This mechanism operates over a broad range of frequencies and is the same basic principle by which microwave ovens heat food.
The relative contributions of resistive and dielectric heating are difficult to separate out experimentally, as tissues are three dimensional and contain various components with different resistances and dielectric properties. It is generally believed that resistive (ionic conduction) heating is the more important mechanism at field strengths used in MRI, while dielectric losses become increasingly important at higher (microwave) frequencies used in electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR).
