Continuous vs Pulsed NMR

Is RF transmitted as a wave or a pulse?
Diagram showing transmit-receive head coil in an MR system.
RF-excitation requires a separate field (B1) rotating/oscillating
near the proton resonant (Larmor) frequency and directed
perpendicularly to the main magnetic field (Bo).
Perturbation of the nuclear spin system by a radiofrequency field is a fundamental requirement for generating the NMR signal. Transmitted into the subject by specialized coils, this RF-field is denoted B1 (to distinguish it from Bo, the main magnetic field). To be effective at imparting energy into the spin system, B1 must be rotating/oscillating near the Larmor (resonance) frequency and have components in the transverse plane. When these conditions are met, the net magnetization (M) can be altered in predictable and useful ways.
The excitation may be in the form of either a long-duration continuous wave (CW) or a short duration RF-pulse. Before the 1950's continuous wave techniques were used exclusively because wide-band RF amplifiers and electronics for precise timing and digitization were not yet available. In the 1950's pulsed techniques became possible due to continuing advances in radar technology (that began in World War II) and newer electronic components. By the mid-1960's pulsed-RF methods had become widespread and by the 1980s had nearly completely replaced continuous wave techniques.
CW NMR showing the development of NMR absorption signal as the magnetic field is s swept from low to high
Continuous Wave (CW) NMR

​The groundbreaking experiments by Rabi, Purcell, and Bloch all used the technique of what would later be called Continuous Wave (CW) NMR.  In this method a low power B1 field is applied for many seconds while the main magnetic field (Bo) is slowly swept from low to high values, revealing absorption signals from each type of nucleus one at at time.  (Alternatively, Bo can be kept constant while the frequency of B1 is swept).
Throughout the 1950s and 1960s CW NMR was the dominant method in use. Gradually, however, pulsed NMR methods prevailed. Limitations and disadvantages of CW NMR that led to its decline included:

  • Low signal of CW NMR (based on very small power difference between absorption and dispersion)
  • Long acquisition times (CW frequency points are acquired serially)
  • Inability to easily manipulate relaxation (T1, T2) and coherence pathways (CW experiments are performed in the steady state)
  • Inability to encode multiple frequencies
  • Limited post-processing options
By the 1980s CW NMR had been nearly completely supplanted by Pulsed NMR.  Its survives today mainly in education, low-field relaxometry, niche materials studies, and hybrid pulsed NMR experiments for proton decoupling, spin locking, and saturation transfer. However, continuous wave techniques remain prominent in a closely related field, electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR). Here because electron spins have much larger magnetic moments and faster relaxation times than nuclear spins, steady-state absorption is strong, rapid, and information-rich.  
Pulsed NMR

​With pulsed NMR, the B1 field is not transmitted continuously, but is only in short bursts. These so-called RF-pulses usually have durations of just a few milliseconds or less. Their magnitude is also small (compared to Bo) — typically from 2−20 μT. 

​RF-pulses (which have a center frequencies in the Larmor range) impart sufficient energy to the spin system to rotate the net magnetization (M) into the transverse plane, generating a signal known as the free induction decay (FID).
A short-duration, rectangular ("hard") RF-pulse that tips M into the transverse plane, generating an FID.
The FID is the simplest of the time-dependent signals recorded in an NMR experiment. Its base oscillation is near the Larmor frequency, but it contains other frequency components arising from varied chemical composition of the sample as well as off-resonance effects, leading to its somewhat shaggy contour. These other components represent important information encoded in the FID signal including spatial location, chemical shifts, and flow.  Because of field inhomogeneities the FID decays exponentially (with a time constant known as T2*) and usually disappears within a few hundred milliseconds in human tissues.
The FID is a time-domain signal which is difficult to interpret in its recorded form. To extract useful spatial and chemical information a mathematical conversion (Fourier Transformation) into the frequency domain is required.  The many details of this process will be covered extensively in later Q&As.
RF-Pulse Characteristics
An RF-pulse typically has a base or carrier frequency centered near the Larmor frequency. Note that when magnetic field gradients are applied along the x-, y-, and/or z-axes, the local resonance frequency at a given spatial location may be altered by several hundred to several thousand Hz. Adjusting the center frequency of the RF-pulse can thus focus its effect on a single slice or restricted group of voxels.  This important concept is the basis of frequency-encoding to be discussed in subsequent Q&A's.
RF-pulses generally contain a narrow range of frequencies on either side of their base.  This is known as the transmitter bandwidth (tBW) that is usually on the order of ±1000 Hz. 
Most RF-pulses in clinical use are amplitude-modulated, but some are also frequency (or phase)-modulated. These forms of modulation have long been known in radio transmission, where they are known as AM and FM respectively.  The shape and nature of this modulation together with gradient-induced variations of the magnetic field will determine which portions of the imaging volume are stimulated.
Left: Amplitude-modulated RF sinc pulse with constant center frequency. Right: Amplitude- and frequency-modulated sinc pulse with changing center frequency. Adiabatic RF pulses are typically of this type.
Primary Applications of RF-Pulses
RF-pulses have three major functions in MRI:
  1. Excitation (saturation), 
  2. Inversion of the net magnetization (M), and
  3. Refocusing of spins to form stronger or more coherent signal.  
For now we will be most concerned with RF-excitation, but will revisit the other aspects of RF-pulses and their design in later sections.
