Safety of Ferumoxytol


How safe is ferumoxytol?  
Ferumoxytol, branded as Feraheme® and Ferabright™, is an iron oxide-containing compound used primarily for treatment of iron deficiency anemia. Because of its unique magnetic relaxation properties, ferumoxytol has found "off-label" application as an MRI contrast agent. In 2025, Ferabright™ was approved by the FDA specifically for MRI adults with suspected brain tumors. 
The typical imaging dose is 3 mg ferumoxytol/kg of body weight, with a range of 1-5 allowed. It should be diluted in a 1:4 ratio with normal saline and injected by slow IV infusion over 15 minutes. Vital signs should be measured before, 5 and 30 minutes after its administration. To identify late reactions, it is avisable for patients be observed an additional 30 minutes in the MRI center after their exam is completed. The need for a slow infusion is highlighted by the FDA “black box warning” issued in 2015, resulting from several deaths and severe reactions to the rapid administration of the drug as a therapeutic agent.

(from Shi under CC-BY)
Overall, ferumoxytol can be considered reasonably safe. The most common reactions, occurring in less than 2% in aggregate, include hypotension, nausea, flushing, backache, pruritus, headache, and vomiting. A few cases of severe anaphyllaxis and severe hypotension have been reported during imaging of both adultss and children. There is no evidence that ferumoxytol causes renal injury as primary excretion is through the liver and bowel.

Although not widely recognized in the imaging literature, extreme care must be exercised during its injection to avoid extravasation. This may result in tattooing or brown skin staining as pictured left that may be irreversible or clear incompletely even several months later.
