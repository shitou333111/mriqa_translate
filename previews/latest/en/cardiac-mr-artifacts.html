Cardiac MR Artifacts

What are the common and unique artifacts observed in cardiac MRI?
Cardiac MRI experiences most of the same artifacts seen in other parts of the body, including those due to flow/motion, aliasing, data processing, chemical shifts, susceptibility, and parallel imaging.  Multiple representative examples are shown below (several from Ferreria et al under CC-BY)

Additionally, there are a few artifacts relatively unique to Cardiac MRI which merit a brief discussion.
Dark Rim Artifact
This artifact is important enough to merit its own Q&A.  It is seen along the endocardium during the first pass of contra-enhanced study and caused by a combination of limited spatial resolution (Gibbs ringing), motion of the heart during imaging, with possible minor contributions from susceptibility of the gadolinium contrast.

Susceptibility Artifacts from Cardiac Devices
Many cardiac-related devices contain metal that produces Bo inhomogeneity and susceptibility artifacts: Cardiac Implantable Electronic Devices (CIEDs) - including both pulse generators and leads; vascular stents; occlusion/diverter devices; and artificial valves. bSSFP sequences are particular sensitive to these effects.
Above: Large area of signal dropout (*) in upper left chest due to pacemaker pulse generator. Black area in right ventricle (green arrow) is susceptibility from a lead.
Right Off-resonance banding
A left-sided pulse generator is usually the biggest source of susceptibility artifacts. As shown below, the artifact may be reduced or shifted by having the patient raise their left arm during image acquisition. Right-sided generators do not generally create such major artifacts over the heart, but images can still be improved by raising the right arm.
Reduction of pulse-generator artifact by ipsilateral arm raising. (From Vuorinen et al. under CC-BY)
Errors in T1 and T2 mapping
Significant errors in T1, T2, and T2* precision may occur without immediately apparent gross visual abnormalities. The most common causes are misalignment between source images, inconsistent slice positioning, imperfect cardiac phase synchronization, and the presence of mis-triggered or skipped heartbeats during data acquisition. 

To address these problems, non-rigid motion correction (MOCO) algorithms are applied to align anatomical structures across source images, primarily correcting for respiratory motion. However, these scheme principally address only in-plane (not through-plane) motion and have limited efficacy in cardiac arrhythmias. 

 As a result, raw and motion-corrected source images, the final map, and available quality control maps (e.g., R² or error maps) should be systematically inspected after acquisition [15]. Most vendor-provided error maps offer a pixel-wise visualization quantifying the fitting error, indicating how well the input pixel values conform to the T1 or T2 equation.
Left: Error map showing high error map reveals high uncertainty in fitted myocardial T1 values and adjacent structures (white = higher uncertainty, black = lower uncertainty). Right: After reacquiring the data at the same slice position with an identical breath-hold position, motion correction was successfully applied. The new error map demonstrates low uncertainty in the myocardial T1 fit.
