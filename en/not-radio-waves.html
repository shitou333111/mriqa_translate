Nature of the MR signal

Isn't the MR signal just re-emission of absorbed radio waves?  
The idea that the MR signal is simply the re-emission or "re-broadcast" by nuclei of radio waves absorbed from the B1 field is a myth. 
Since the discovery of NMR it was recognized that the signal produced by a single nuclear transition would be far too weak to detect with macroscopic instrumentation. The coupling between a single nuclear magnetic moment and a receiver coil is extraordinarily small, and the spontaneous emission rate of nuclear spins at radio frequencies is extremely slow. If NMR signals depended on individual nuclei radiating photons independently, the resulting emission would be far too weak and too randomly phased to produce a measurable signal.

In 1954 Robert Dicke introduced the concept of cooperative spontaneous emission (later called superradiance), in which many quantum emitters coupled to the same electromagnetic mode can radiate collectively and produce an enhanced burst of radiation. Some researchers suggested that a similar cooperative mechanism might explain how ensembles of nuclear spins generate detectable NMR signals.
​
Beginning in the late 1980s, however, David Hoult carefully reexamined this issue and showed that the observed NMR signal can be explained entirely by the classical electrodynamics of a precessing magnetization coupled to a resonant detection circuit. In this view the rotating macroscopic magnetization induces an EMF in the receiver coil according to Faraday’s law. The cooperative spontaneous-emission picture is therefore unnecessary and, in some formulations, inconsistent with the measured signal strength.
​
It is therefore misleading to think of nuclei in an NMR experiment as tiny radio transmitters radiating photons independently. A more accurate description is that the collective transverse magnetization of the spin ensemble couples inductively to the detection coil, producing the MR signal through classical electromagnetic induction.
