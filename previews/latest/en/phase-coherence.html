Phase Coherence

Why are all the spins brought into phase with one another after a
​90°-pulse?  
As written, the question has some hidden interpretive traps. As written, the question seems to be tacitly assuming that before the pulse the spins had random but well-defined phases and the pulse synchronized them.  This is incorrect.
​
Before RF excitation, an equilibrium spin does not possess a definite transverse phase that is merely unknown; it is in a mixed quantum state with no transverse coherence, so the concept of an individual spin phase at this time is undefined. The spins are not all "out of phase"; they simply have no phase.

The 90°-pulse creates transverse coherence, placing the spins in a common phase reference defined by the RF field. The RF pulse does not take precessing spins and lock them together.  It creates transverse coherence from longitudinal polarization.

It can be shown by relatively straightforward quantum mechanical arguments that a homogeneous RF-field, acting on a population of non-interacting spins without relaxation produces identical unitary evolution of all spins and therefore does not change their relative orientations. In other words, an RF-field can only rotate the spin system as a whole (and thus its net magnetization, M). This, of course, is an idealized situation applicable primarily to pulsed NMR experiments and requires a number of assumptions (e.g., spatial uniformity of the ​Bo and B1 fields, on-resonance excitation with negligible chemical shift dispersion, non-interacting spins, and a very-short RF pulse allowing no time for T1 or T2 relaxation to occur).
Although in real life all of these assumptions may be violated to some degree, the ideal model still reflects fairly closely what is actually happening and provides a useful basis for understanding the NMR phenomenon in pulsed RF experiments.
The best way to understand this difficult concept, I believe, is to return to a classic (but imperfect) macroscopic analogy used in a prior Q&A, "Compasses in a Dryer". 
Compasses in the Dryer Analogy
A 90° pulse applies a unitary rotation to the spin system, tipping the longitudinal magnetization Mz into the transverse plane. In doing so, it converts the small thermodynamic polarization (Zeeman order) present before the pulse into an equivalent amount of transverse quantum coherence.
​In this analogy, individual spinning nuclei were likened to tiny compass needles each seeking to point to the north pole (symbolized by globe with its main magnetic field Bo pointing upward). Because of thermal interactions (the dryer is hot and spinning), the individual compass needles ended up pointing in all possible directions. On the average, however, more were pointing north than in any other direction. When we summed them all together, we obtained M, the net magnetization (technically, a statistical "expectation") that pointed due North.

At equilibrium prior to the RF-pulse the spin system has a net population difference representing a form of thermodynamic "order". The only effect of the 90°-pulse is to rotate this entire spin-distribution (and therefore M) into the transverse plane.  Thus, the small Boltzmann population imbalance between the Zeeman energy levels along z- is converted by the RF pulse into transverse phase coherence.
​
An ideal 90°-pulse would be expected to change the phases of all precessing spins in the sample. RF pulses convert the small initial Boltzmann population imbalance into transverse magnetization. All spins remain phase-coherent, but only this small polarized component contributes to the net vector M. The much larger unpolarized background produces no signal because it cancels in the ensemble average. ​The NMR signal is small not because most spins are random, but because most of the spin ensemble is quantum-mechanically invisible.
Only the small population imbalance between the Zeeman energy levels produced by the Boltzmann distribution contributes to the net NMR signal. Observable transverse magnetization arises from this initial population difference, which is converted into coherent transverse magnetization by RF excitation. The much larger number of spins that are equally populated in the two levels form a background that does not contribute to the net signal, because their contributions cancel in the ensemble average.​
Using somewhat more obtuse terminology from quantum mechanics:
  • Before the RF-pulse spin ensemble has a small but measurable net magnetization (M) directed along the +z-axis (due to the Boltzmann population imbalance)
  • In the absence of transverse coherence, individual spins do not possess well-defined transverse phases
  • An idealized 90°-pulse applies the same unitary rotation to entire spin ensemble (or equivalently, the spin density matrix) by 90° about the axis (x- or y-) defined by the RF phase 
  • ​As a result, all spins acquire a common transverse phase and become phase-coherent. 
  • The small longitudinal polarization is thereby converted into transverse coherence
  • This results in a nonzero transverse magnetization equal in magnitude to the original Mz
In summary: In the ideal theoretical model, an RF-pulse does not impose an arbitrary absolute transverse phase on each spin individually; rather, it applies the same unitary rotation operator to all spins. This produces transverse magnetization whose phase is globally defined by the RF field, without introducing any new relative phase differences among the spins.

From a quantum mechanical perspective, recall from a previous Q&A that the wave function describing the hydrogen nucleus could be written as:

Ψ(t) = a |+½⟩ + b |-½⟩

where a and b are time-dependent quantum amplitudes incorporating "harmonic oscillations" of the two principal states |+½⟩ and |-½⟩.

When an RF field is applied at the Larmor frequency, its action on the wave function Ψ(t) is to induce a "rotation" or mixing of the a and b components while preserving their total amplitudes (|a|² + |b|²). This is the quantum mechanical explanation for why the transverse magnetization after a 90°-pulse is the same as the longitudinal magnetization before the pulse. It is also the reason that this same amount of magnetization, but no more, can be rotated to 180° or any other angle in an NMR fast passage experiment. In an odd sort of way, therefore, the RF-field really does "rotate" the magnetic moment of a proton, but in a manner much more complex and beautiful than the tipping of a spinning top.
