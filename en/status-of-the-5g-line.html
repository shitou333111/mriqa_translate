The 5 Gauss Line

Whatever happened to the 5 gauss line for MR safety? 
Read MR Safety Disclaimer Before Proceeding
The 5 gauss line is history. 9G is the new 5G.
For the last 40 years the 5 gauss (0.5 mT) line has been used to demarcate the maximum allowable fringe field safety threshold around an MR scanner. Numerous anecdotes exist concerning the origin of this value, but it most likely became established in 1983 at an MRI regulatory panel of the US Food and Drug Administration. The perceived need for a limit was based on the fact that early generation cardiac pacemakers had magnetically-sensitive reed switches that could be affected by fields down to about 10 gauss (1.0 mT), so a value half this size was chosen as the threshold.
The global authority recognized in over 170 countries to develop and publishes standard for all electrical, electronic, and related technologies is the International Electrotechnical Commission, headquartered in Geneva, Switzerland. Concerning MRI safety and exposure, the relevant international standard is IEC 60601-2-33. All previous versions of that document had recommended limiting fringe exposures to 5 gauss or less, but in 2022 (Edition 4.0) that value was increased to 9 gauss (0.9 mT). Subsequently, major MRI professional bodies such as the American College of Radiology, Medicines and Healthcare Products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) in the UK, and Royal Australian and New Zealand
College of Radiologists (RANZCR), have moved to adopt this new threshold.  
It is not anticipated that patient safety will be adversely affected by this change.  Note that 0.9 mT is still well outside the "projectile zone" which does not begin until at least 3.0 mT for even the most strongly ferromagnetic objects.  Furthermore, the cost savings of site design (i.e., not requiring additional fringe shielding) may prove significant, particularly with high-field and ultra-high-field scanners.
