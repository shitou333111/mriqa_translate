Wave Properties

What properties define a electromagnetic wave?
The periodically oscillating function that describes the shape of an electromagnetic wave has three fundamental properties explicitly apparent in its mathematical formulation:
S(t) = A sin(ωot − ϕ)
where S(t) is the signal as a function of time, A is the amplitude, ωo is the angular frequency, and ϕ is the instantaneous phase.  
Frequency (f and ω)
As some readers may not be familiar with angular frequency, a brief YouTube video and explanation may be in order
Regular or linear frequency (f), sometimes also denoted by the Greek symbol "nu" (ν), counts the number of complete oscillations or rotations in a given period of time. Its units are therefore cycles per second (cps), also called hertz (Hz). Frequencies used in MRI are typically between 10 and 300 MHz ("mega-hertz" = million Hz).

Angular frequency (ω), also known as radial or circular frequency, measures angular displacement per unit time. Its units are therefore degrees (or radians) per second. Since one full cycle around a circle is 2π radians = 360°, 1 radian ≈ 57.3°. The magnitude of angular frequency (in radians) is larger than that of regular frequency (in Hz) by a factor of 2π:                 ω = 2πf
Velocity (v)
In a vacuum electromagnetic waves travel at the speed of light, commonly denoted by "c". This velocity is a universal physical constant exactly equal to 299,792,458 m/s. (The number is exact, because by international agreement, a meter is defined in terms of the speed of light).

In matter the velocity slows because the oscillating electric field polarizes bound charges (electrons and ions) producing induced electric dipoles throughout the material. These dipoles re-radiate secondary fields that are out of phase with the incident wave. The superposition of the incident and reradiated fields leads to a phase lag and a reduced phase velocity, so that the wavefront to propagates more slowly through the material than in a vacuum. (This phenomenon is the basis of the the so-called refractive index in physics).

The electromagnetic response of a material is characterized by both its relative (electric) permittivity (εr) and its relative (magnetic) permeability (μr). In non-magnetic, non-dispersive media, Maxwell’s equations predict that electromagnetic waves propagate with phase velocity v = c (εrμr)−½. For most biological tissues μr ≈ 1 so the εr component dominates. Human tissues are moderately dielectric (with εr ≈ 60−80), so without considering conductivity losses, the velocity of RF waves in the body is only about 11−13% of the speed of light. However, in real tissues at RF frequencies (10-300 MHz), permittivity is frequency-dependent and complex, and conductive losses may be significant. As a result, the wave is strongly attenuated and and distorted with phase velocity perhaps as high as 0.2c (depending on the exact frequency and tissue type).

Wavelength (λ)
Wavelength (λ) is defined as the distance between corresponding points (e.g., peaks or troughs) of a sinusoidal wave with velocity (v) and frequency (f) and connected by the formula: λ = v / f.  Because of electrical permittivity and conductivity effects, the wavelength of RF waves in human tissues, like velocity, is only about 10-20% of those in a vacuum.
At 3.0 T and higher RF-wavelengths become comparable in size to body cross-sectional dimensions. The implications include: 1) significantly increased B1 field inhomogeneity; 2) the appearance of standing wave effects with constructive/destructive interference, and 3) dielectric shading.

Phase (ϕ)
The concept of instantaneous phase and its relation to frequency can be another confusing topic. Instantaneous phase (ϕ) represents an angular shift of a periodic wave from its origin. For example, a sine wave with ϕ = 0 should have a value of zero at t = 0.  The sine wave pictured above has been phase-shifted and has a non-zero value. Phase shifts are measured in radians (or degrees).  Under this definition, a sine wave and a cosine wave are 90° (π/2 radians) out of phase with each other.

After a period of time, Δt, two sine waves initially synchronized in phase but differing in frequency by Δω radians per second will develop a differential total phase shift (ΔΦ) given by: ΔΦ = Δω × Δt. Or in more general terms expressed by calculus: 
Φ = ∫ ω(t) dt
where Φ is the total phase shift accumulated over an increment of time (Δt≅dt) and ω(t) is the frequency that may vary as a function of time. The total accumulated phase shift (Φ) can be thought of as the area under a frequency vs time curve. This fact will prove useful when we discuss the phase changes induced by gradients or flow in later Q&A's. 
If you still don't quite understand why (frequency x time) = phase, think about the units of measurement. Frequency is commonly measured in Hertz, or cycles per second. Time is measured in seconds.  So frequency x time = (cycles/sec) x sec = # of cycles. Thus two sine waves differing in frequency by 200 Hz get progressively out of phase with each other by 200 cycles every second.
To understand the more complex aspects of frequency and phase encoding of the MR image, it is necessary to review what happens when two sine waves are added together.  If two sine waves have the same frequency but different phases, their summation is another sine wave with the same base frequency but a different amplitude and phase.  When the two waves are perfectly in phase with each other, their signals augment each other.  When they are slightly out of phase with each other, the overall signal is diminished, and they are said to destructively interfere.  When the phase shift between them reaches 180°, the two waves exactly cancel each other. 
Summation of two sine waves of the same frequency but different phases. As Δφ → 180° (out-of-phase condition) the two waves destructively interfere, yielding a net signal that is nearly zero. (Courtesy of Dr. Dan Russell, Penn State Grad Prog in Acoustics).
