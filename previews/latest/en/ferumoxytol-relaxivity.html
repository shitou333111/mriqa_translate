Ferumoxytol Relaxivity

How the relaxivity of ferumoxytol compare to gadolinium based contrast agent?  
Ferumoxytol (marketed as Feraheme® in North America and Rienso® elsewhere) is an ultrasmall superparamagnetic iron oxide (USPIO) approved for intravenous treatment of iron-deficiency anemia associated with chronic renal disease. Because it has properties similar to previously approved but no longer commercially available USPIOs, ferumoxytol in now being used "off-label" by many MR centers for imaging of the liver, lymphatic system, bone marrow, inflammatory diseases, tumor necrosis, perfusion imaging, MR angiography, cardiac viability, and plaque characterization. In late 2025, the FDA approved a ferumoxytol contrast agent (Ferabright™) for cranial imaging in adults with suspected brain tumors, discussed in more detail in a later Q&A.
Intravenously injected ferumoxytol initially distributes in the intravacular space but does not bind to plasma proteins nor enter erythrocytes. Kidney filtration is bypassed due the particles' thick carbohydrate coating, resulting in prolonged intravascular residence time (~14½ hrs). In solid organs USPIOs are confined to the intravascular space, but will slowly leak across a disrupted blood-brain barrier or hyper-permeable endothelium of tumor microvessels. Ultimately they are phagocytosed by macrophages and their iron recirculated.
USPIOs typically reduce both T1 and T2/T2* values of tissues where they accumulate, but the net effect on MR signal dependens on particle size, concentration, and microscopic compartmentalization. In plasma, ferumoxytol particles have easy access to water protons, and hence produce intravascular enhancement on T1-weighted images. Aggregation of particles in tissues typically results in more T2/T2* shortening than T1 effects. Knobloch et al (2018) measured the r1, r2, and r2* relaxivities of ferumoxytol in saline, plasma, and blood at 1.5T and 3.0T over a range of expected physiological concentrations. As a function of increasing field strength, T1 relaxivity (r1) decreased by about half while T2/T2* relaxivites (r2, r2*) were relatively unchanged. Compared to most gadolinium-based contrast agents (that possess r1 and r2 values in the range of 5-10 L/mmol-s), ferumoxytol produced T1-relaxation at levels 3 times that of an equivalent dose of gadolinium contrast, while T2 relaxivity was up to 25-fold greater.
