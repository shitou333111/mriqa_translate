MR Safety: SAR Measurements

How do MRI scanners measure SAR during a scan? 
​MRI scanners do not measure SAR directly in the patient. Instead, they estimate it in real time using a combination of RF power monitoring, electromagnetic models, and patient data. The result is a conservative prediction designed to stay within regulatory limits set by agencies like the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) and US Food and Drug Administration (FDA).  Manufacturers must demonstrate that their scanners can predict and limit SAR according to these standards.
RF meter showing forward and reverse (reflected) power
The scanner continuously monitors both forward RF power (sent to the coil) and reverse RF power (reflected from coil due to impedance mismatch).  Using directional couplers, the system computes the difference between forward and reflected power which is the actual RF power entering the coil-patient system.

Based on the MR pulse sequence selected or run, the scanner knows all the critical adjustable parameters (flip angle, TR/TE, duty cycle, RF-pulse magnitude/duration/shape, number of slices, etc) allowing it to calculate the RF energy administered per pulse. 
Built into the scanner's database are look-up tables that relate RF input power to local and global SAR. These tables are based on full-wave Maxwell simulations in thousands of scenarios involving human anatomy models with different sizes and positions using different coils and frequencies. When the tech enters the demographic information for a patient in the scanner (weight, height, exam region, coil type, etc), the scanner estimates effective body mass, coil loading, and coupling.  It then selects the closest matching model from the database. 
A whole body SAR is then computed as the predicted absorbed RF power ÷ patient mass.  This provides a global heating risk and is updated continuously during the scan. If the predicted SAR exceeds regulatory limits, then the scanner will not allow the sequence to be run and suggest parameter tradeoffs to keep SAR values within range.
Because local SARs cannot be measured, the scanner makes a conservative estimate of local (10g) SARs by tracking coil sensitivity maps, B1 field patterns, and current sequence parameters. "Worst reasonable case" hot-spots are identified. Manufacturers deliberately overestimate SAR values for safety reasons.
In scanners that utilize multi-channel (parallel) RF transmission, the calculation of SAR is more difficult. This is because each channel makes its own SAR contribution, interference patterns occur, and hot spots become highly unpredictable. Such systems utilize real-time SAR matrices, channel power tracking, and subject-specific calibration.
