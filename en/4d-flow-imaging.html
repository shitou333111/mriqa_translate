4D Flow Imaging

What is the 4th dimension in 4D Flow imaging?  
The 4th dimension is time.  So 4D Flow Imaging is time-resolved, phase-contrast imaging over a (3D) vascular volume. Original applications of this technology focused on the aortic flow velocities, but more recently has been expanded to other cardiac and vascular indications, as well as flow of cerebrospinal fluid, 
The typical baseline pulse sequence for 4D flow imaging is a 3D-spoiled gradient echo sequence with short TR and TE and flip angle near the Ernst angle (~7° for non-contrast studies). Contrast is not generally needed, but if given for other reasons may allow a reduction in scan time and improved signal-to-noise. Phase-based flow-encoding is performed by consecutively applying gradients in three orthogonal directions. The protocol is actually a four-point method, as one additional velocity insensitive reference acquisition is obtained to remove phase errors from other sources.

​Processing and analysis of the flow data requires sophisticated tools, most of which are now supplied by the scanner manufacturer (e.g., Siemens, GE). Third-party options, such as 4D-flow tool of CVI42™ (Circle Cardiovascular Imaging, Calgary) or iTFlow™ (Cardio Flow Design, Tokyo) are also available.
In addition to simple blood velocity measurements, 4D Flow methods create opportunities to explore a number of advanced metrics with interesting clinical and research possibilities.  These include:
  • Flow displacement: deviation of flow from the aortic centerline or middle of a cardiac valve, allowing quantification of outflow asymmetry.
  • Wall shear stress (WSS): the tangential force per unit area exerted by blood flow on the vessel wall due to frictional drag of blood moving along the endothelium
  • Oscillatory shear index (OSI):  directional changes in the WSS over the cardiac cycle, indicating regions of disturbed flow that may potentiate atherosclerosis or aneurysm formation
  • Relative residence time (RRT): measures the time that blood elements spend in a given region, an index for areas prone to thrombosis and plaque formation
  • Mean kinetic energy; the average energy of particles in fluid calculated using the phase-averaged velocity of the fluid flow. Energy loss may occur in turbulence. 
  • Pulse wave velocity (PWV):  a measure of arterial stiffness and is computed  by analyzing the transit time of blood flow pulses along a vessel (influenced by the elasticity and structural integrity of the vessel). 
  • Vascular distensibility:  calculated as the relative change in the cross-sectional diameter (or area) in a target vessel for a given pressure step.

5D flow imaging is an expansion of standard 4D flow MRI, which captures 3D spatial and time-resolved blood flow but does not resolve respiratory motion. The five dimensions refer to 3D spatial + time + respiration. The 5D pulse sequence includes free-running 3D radial sampling combined with cardiac and respiratory self-gating. (The latter eliminates the need for external gating devices). This allows for simultaneous resolution of both cardiac and respiratory motion effects on hemodynamics throughout the entire heart and vessels in a reasonable time frame (< 8 minutes). Such techniques may offer improved evaluation of how respiration impacts flow dynamics in cases of vascular stenosis, shunts, and congenital cardiac malformations.
