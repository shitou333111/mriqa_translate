RF-pulses beyond 90° 

I think I understand how 90°-pulses work. But what happens when extended to 180°, 270°, or even 360°?
Rotation of net magnetization from 0° to 360° by an RF pulse.
Rotation from 90°​ to 180°
​
The rotation of M from 90° to 180° requires continued input of RF energy into to the spin system. Assuming this has been accomplished by a short-duration, ideal RF pulse, the rotation is coherent and spin evolution remains unitary and reversible. As with a 90°-pulse, no population redistribution occurs. The Boltzmann populations are preserved in magnitude but exchanged between the Zeeman levels. The RF pulse therefore reorients the macroscopic magnetization vector without changing the entropy or equilibrating the Zeeman levels. 
After a perfect 180° pulse, M is inverted with respect to Bo and its longitudinal component (Mz) now equals −Mo (the same magnitude but sign opposite to its initial value +Mo).  The Zeeman expectation energy is correspondingly raised from −MoBo to +MoBo, placing the spin system in a metastable inverted state. This change reflects a coherent rearrangement of populations produced by unitary evolution, not the irreversible absorption of energy. No entropy is generated. Any pre-existing transverse coherences are preserved up to rotation. The excess Zeeman energy is released only during subsequent longitudinal (T1) relaxation.
​In a short, non-saturating 180°-RF pulse, the added RF energy is stored reversibly as increased Zeeman energy in the inverted spin populations—that is, in the Zeeman reservoir—until it is later dissipated to the lattice by T₁ relaxation.
At 180° the spins now occupy stationary energy eigenstates, but in proportions that maximize rather than minimize their magnetic potential energy in the static field. This inverted distribution represents stored energy, not in the form of oscillatory coherence, but as excess occupation of energetically unfavorable states.
Like a compass needle forced to point in the wrong direction, this inverted state of M is metastable and cannot persist indefinitely.  Two scenarios are may occur:
  • T1 relaxation will result in energy flow from the spin system into the environment ("lattice"), causing a regrowth of M purely along the z-axis until it returns to its initial value of +Mo.
  • Continued RF-pulse stimulation will produce further rotation beyond 180° to 270°, 360° or beyond.
Regrowth of M along the z-axis after a 180°-pulse due to T1-relaxation.
Rotation from 180° to 270°
Recall that Zeeman energy refers to the energy of interaction between a magnetic moment (M) and an external magnetic field (Bo).  Since the Bo is typically assigned to lie along the z-axis, its Zeeman energy (Ez) can be written as Ez = − M • Bo  = − MoBo.  As shown in the diagram, the Zeeman energies for magnetizations at  0° /  90° / 180° /  270° / 360°  are  −MoBo / 0 / +MoBo ​/ 0 / −​MoBo. The energy difference between 0° and 180° is therefore +MoBo − (−MoBo ) = 2​MoBo.
​For an ideal on-resonance pulse, continued rotation beyond 180° involves ongoing coherent energy exchange between the RF field and the spin system. At 270° the transverse magnetization is again maximal (along −y), and the Zeeman energy is zero. This state is the phase-inverted analogue of the 90° state. The transition from 180° to 270° corresponds to a reduction in Zeeman energy equal to MoBo.  Because no relaxation occurs during the short pulse, this energy cannot be dissipated as heat; instead it is returned coherently to the RF field and ultimately to the coupled RF circuitry.  Details of the coupling mechanism can be found in this related Q&A. 
Continued rotation returns the magnetization to the +z direction.  After completion of a 360°-RF pulse, the density matrix, magnetization, and internal energy are identical to their initial values. During the pulse, energy is coherently exchanged between the RF field and the spin system, reaching a maximum at 180°, but all of this energy is returned to the RF circuitry by the end of the cycle. No transverse coherence remains, and no net energy is transferred in an ideal 0°–360° rotation.
