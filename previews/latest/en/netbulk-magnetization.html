Net Magnetization (M)

What is net magnetization and how does it apply to NMR?  
Fortunately, nearly all the ideas behind MRI can be explained in terms of classical physics. Having an appreciation for quantum mechanics adds a certain perspective but is largely unnecessary.

Although particle physicists must address the quantum world regularly, those involved with clinical or research MRI seldom do. The reason is straightforward —  During an MRI experiment we are not looking at the signal from individual nuclei, but from millions to billions in the aggregate. 
As shown in the diagram right, it is preferable not to dwell on individual nuclei (with their "freaky" quantum behavior).  Instead I recommend you think about them as multiple classic magnetic dipoles. Quantum mechanics precludes us from having too much a priori knowledge about the individual µi's. For example, without a measurement we cannot simultaneously know their angular momentum in a given direction or their phase relation to other spins.

​Although we know little about individual dipoles, we can understand their aggregate behavior. We do this by adding their magnetic moments to create an entity known as net magnetization, M. 
Net magnetization (M), the averaged sum of many individual quantum spins, can be treated as a regular vector in classical physics.
Net magnetization (M), sometimes referred to as bulk magnetization, behaves as a "regular" vector using the principles of classical physics.  From this point forth we will almost exclusively use the vector M rather than individual nuclear spins when explaining aspects of NMR and MRI.
At thermal equilibrium (i.e., before RF-excitation begins) M is typically is aligned with the main magnetic field (Bo).  In the standard laboratory coordinate system the Bo field is considered to lie along the z-axis. Once MR imaging begins, radiofrequency pulses cause M to be tipped away from its initial alignment. M can be considered to have both longitudinal (Mz) and transverse (Mxy) components.

In more formal analysis, the net magnetization vector (M) now operating under the laws of classical physics is considered the expectation taken over the entire ensemble. When the results of an experiment depend only upon the statistical behavior of a large number of quantized particles, an explanation in terms of classical (non-quantum) physics is generally possible. This is indeed the case with NMR, in which the expectation value of the magnetization may be shown to have any orientation to the magnetic field, ranging from parallel to antiparallel and including all values between.
