T1-rho (T1ρ)

What is T1-rho and how does it differ from T1 and T2?
T1-rho (T1ρ) is a measure of the decay of transverse magnetization under very special experimental conditions -- when the magnetization is "locked" by a continuous, low-frequency RF pulse during its evolution. By comparison, conventional T1 and T2 relaxation times are measured in the absence of RF-excitation. Additionally, unlike T1 and T2, the measured value of T1ρ is not a single value, but influenced by the spin-lock pulse's amplitude and duration.

A typical T1ρ spin-lock experiment is shown below.  After an initial short 90°-RF pulse tips spins into the transverse plane, a second low-amplitude, continuous RF-pulse (the “spin-lock” pulse) is applied along the direction of the transverse magnetization. The T1ρ-time characterizes how quickly this “locked” transverse magnetization decays to zero.
(From Bustin et al under CC-BY-4.0)
T1ρ dispersion measurements in biological tissues and models have demonstrated that ¹H-¹H proton exchange is the dominant relaxation mechanism 0.1 - 10kHz spin-lock range (typical used for most clinical scanners). T1ρ is sensitive to slower molecular processes and interactions between water and macromolecules, making it particularly useful to detect changes in tissue composition, like early cartilage degeneration, fibrosis, or pH changes.  In clinical applications, T1ρ measurements and mapping have found greatest utility in musculoskeletal and cardiac imaging.
