Whole and Half Integers for I

Why are the allowable values for spin either whole or half integers? Why couldn't all the spins be whole numbers, or for that matter, any value whatsoever? 
  
The fundamental tenet of quantum mechanics is that many basic phenomena at the atomic level (such as energy emission and angular momentum) appear to be quantized.  “Quantized” means that when certain properties of a system are measured, a continuous range of results are not observed, but rather a set of discrete (separate) values.  If “quanta” come in discrete units then it makes sense to count them with integers. 
The proposal that the electron had intrinsic spin-½ by Uhlenbeck and Goudsmit in 1925 was initially met with skepticism, largely because it appeared classically implausible and lacked a fundamental theoretical basis. Although half-integer quantum numbers were already being used in spectroscopy and Pauli had incorporated spin-½ into quantum mechanics, it was Paul Dirac’s 1928 relativistic theory of the electron that provided the first fundamental derivation of spin-½ and firmly established its physical necessity.
Some appreciation for why both integer and half-integer quantum numbers crop up may be gained by considering the properties of simple standing waves, such as vibrations of a guitar string. It is well recognized that standing waves only occur when their harmonics are either whole or half-integral multiples of the string length. 

The guitar string example is only an analogy, not a proof, of why both whole and half-integer spin values might be reasonable at the quantum level.  I present this to provide you with some comfort that all quantum features are not entirely foreign to our experience in the macroscopic world.
Harmonic vibrations of a string follow a progression of wavelengths (λ) similar to spin quantum numbers: 1/2, 1, 3/2, 2, 5/2, etc.

In quantum mechanics, the possible values of angular momentum are not arbitrary but are tightly constrained by how physical systems must behave under rotations. Ordinary space is symmetric under three-dimensional rotations, and any quantum state must transform consistently under these operations. Mathematically, this symmetry is encoded in the Lie rotation group SO(3) and, more fundamentally for quantum systems, in its related group SU(2). When physicists analyze how quantum states can represent these rotational symmetries, they find that only certain “representations” are allowed. These representations come in discrete families labeled by a number s, which can take the values 0, 1/2, 1, 3/2, and so on. Each value corresponds to a possible type of angular momentum. Thus, quantum mechanics itself does not permit a continuous range of spins; it allows only these specific, quantized possibilities.

A crucial difference between classical and quantum rotations is that quantum states are complex objects called spinors, not ordinary vectors. Under a full 360° rotation, a classical object returns to its original state. A spinor, however, may change sign and only return to itself after a 720° rotation. This unusual behavior is a direct consequence of the mathematical structure of SU(2), which “covers” the classical rotation group in a special way. The simplest nontrivial representation of SU(2) is two-dimensional and corresponds to spin-½. In this representation, a particle has exactly two internal states (often called “up” and “down”), and its wavefunction transforms as a spinor rather than as a classical vector. Higher representations exist and correspond to spin-1, spin-3/2, and so on, but spin-½ is the smallest possible nonzero case.

The fact that a spinor requires a 720° rotation to return to its original state can be understood by analogy to a Möbius strip. Unlike a regular circular strip of paper, drawing a line on a Möbius strip requires two complete revolutions of the paper to return to its beginning.

Electrons and protons are spin-½ particles because they occupy this simplest nontrivial representation of rotational symmetry in quantum mechanics. For electrons, this emerges naturally from relativistic quantum theory: Dirac’s equation, which combines quantum mechanics with special relativity, automatically produces spin-½ states and no others. Protons, though composite particles, inherit the same spin through the combined behavior of their constituent quarks. In this sense, half-integer spin is not an added assumption but a consequence of requiring quantum states to transform consistently under rotations. Spin-½ reflects the most elementary way that a quantum particle can carry angular momentum while respecting the deep geometric structure of space and quantum theory.
