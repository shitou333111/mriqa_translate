Energy Storage

Where the energy added by an RF-pulse physically stored?
​Energy added to the spin system by RF excitation is stored primarily as altered Zeeman order, transverse coherence, or some combination of the two. The partition of energy between these reservoirs depends on the flip angle of the RF pulse. Although the total energy of the spin system increases, no thermal excitation or Boltzmann redistribution occurs. Instead, the RF field produces a coherent redistribution of spin polarization, exchanging population differences and phase-coherent transverse magnetization.

Energy Stored as Zeeman Order
When an RF pulse rotates the spin ensemble (equivalently, when the density matrix undergoes a unitary rotation), the longitudinal magnetization Mz​ progressively decreases and becomes inverted at a 180° flip angle. The spin system is thereby driven into a non-equilibrium population distribution in which the higher-energy Zeeman level becomes more populated than the lower one. This altered Zeeman order represents a static but thermodynamically unstable configuration. The population inversion corresponds to stored energy in the form of excess occupation of energetically unfavorable spin states.
​Physically, this stored energy corresponds to magnetic potential energy associated with excess population of spin states whose magnetic moments are oriented against the applied field. As long as this non-equilibrium Zeeman population distribution persists, the additional energy remains stored within the spin system. Through longitudinal relaxation (T1), however, the spins gradually return to thermal equilibrium by transferring this excess energy to their surroundings. Fluctuating magnetic fields produced by molecular motion—such as lattice vibrations and other electromagnetic fluctuations—drive transitions between the Zeeman levels, restoring the equilibrium population distribution.
Energy Stored as Transverse Coherence
After a 90°-RF pulse (or whenever the net magnetization has transverse components), a portion of the absorbed RF-energy is stored transiently as transverse spin coherence. From a semiclassical perspective, the rotating transverse magnetization can be viewed as a magnetic dipole oscillating at the Larmor frequency. Although most spins belong to nearly equal populations of the two Zeeman states and therefore produce cancelling contributions, the small population imbalance that has been brought into phase gives rise to a macroscopic transverse magnetization Mxy. This coherent ensemble behaves like a collection of microscopic RF current sources whose precessing magnetic moments induce an oscillating voltage in the receiver coil, producing the detectable NMR signal. 
In quantum-mechanical terms, the RF-pulse drives the spin system into a coherent superposition of Zeeman energy eigenstates. It converts the initial Zeeman population spin-up/spin-down imbalance into off-diagonal elements of the density matrix. These off-diagonal terms represent transverse coherence and correspond to a collective oscillation of the spin ensemble at the Larmor frequency. This coherent oscillation reflects stored energy: the spins are no longer in stationary energy states, but in a phase-locked superposition that evolves in time.
​Physically, this stored energy can be viewed as the energy required to maintain the spins in a configuration that is not aligned with the static magnetic field. Instead of occupying the lowest-energy orientation along Bo​, the magnetic moments are maintained in a phase-coherent state that causes them to precess collectively in the transverse plane. As long as this coherence is preserved, the energy remains stored in the ordered motion of the ensemble. When phase coherence is lost through dephasing, the macroscopic transverse magnetization disappears even though the underlying spin energy may remain unchanged. Through longitudinal relaxation (T₁), however, the excess energy stored in the spin system is transferred to the surrounding environment. During signal detection, a small portion of the coherent transverse magnetization can also return energy to the electromagnetic field through inductive coupling, producing the observable NMR signal.
Power Flow During RF Excitation: Coherence as a "Conduit"
The partitioning between transverse coherence and Zeeman order is not a metaphor, but the exact structure of spin dynamics. The spin system after a pulse always lies on a circle (in Hilbert space) connecting pure population order to pure coherence. The flip angle determines where it sits. The partitioning is physically meaningful, because the two components relax independently: Mz via T1 and Mxy via T2 mechanisms respectively (described in later Q&As).
One way to look at this is to regard transverse coherence as the channel through which the RF field does work on the spins, while the net absorbed energy (after the pulse is over) is set by how much the longitudinal Zeeman energy has changed. In other words, the instantaneous power being transferred into (or out of) Zeeman energy is proportional to a transverse expectation value—i.e., to coherence. During nutation, the RF field changes the Zeeman populations only because the state develops transverse coherence; without a transverse component, there is no torque and no energy transfer.
The transverse coherence remaining after the pulse is over (Mxy) does not appear as a static Zeeman potential-energy term (because Ez depends only on Mz). However, it represents non-stationary order that can subsequently be converted into detectable RF emission (as a free induction decay signal) and/or dissipated by dephasing/relaxation.

In brief: Coherence mediates the power flow during the pulse; the final Mz tells you how much energy remains stored as Zeeman order afterward.

A Third but Smaller Reservoir: Energy in the Electromagnetic Field
During and immediately after an RF-pulse, the precessing magnetization (M) produces a localized, oscillatory magnetic field in the surrounding space: the electromagnetic near field of the spin system.This is not a separate reservoir of energy created by the coil, but a local field generated by the coherently precessing spins themselves.
The interactions between spins and fields can be analyzed by classical electromagnetic (EM) theory, standard quantum mechanics (QM), or the advanced methods of quantum electrodynamics (QED). I will try to use a sprinkling of each approach to provide some global insight into the phenomena.

In NMR theory, two electromagnetic regions are commonly distinguished — near field and far field — based on wavelength (λ) relative to the distance (r) of the sample or object studied.
  • If r ≪ λ you are considered to be operating in the Near (Reactive) Zone. Here generated electromagnetic fields do not propagate as waves and there is no significant distant radiation observed. Energy oscillates locally and there is strong coupling between the source and receiver.
  • When r ≫ λ you are in the Far (Radiative) Zone.  Here fields do propagate as waves and energy radiates outward. Only weak coupling exists with the receiver coil and the source (sample) acts like an antenna.
Because wavelengths typically used in NMR and MRI are on the order of 1-5 m, and the receiver coils are placed close to the sample or subject, NMR/MRI operate primarily in the Near Zone. This means that RF interactions of coil and sample have a quasi-static character and are based mainly on induction magnetic fields.
In the immediate vicinity of the dipole, the field is mostly non-radiative and is considered a so-called near field. This field is both produced by the precession of coherent spins and interacts with them, so part of the stored spin energy is literally inside the surrounding EM field.
Transverse magnetization drives an oscillating magnetization current that injects electromagnetic energy into the near field; Poynting’s vector carries this energy to the RF coil, where it is dissipated, exactly balancing the loss of coherent spin energy.
