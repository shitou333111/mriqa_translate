Cross-talk

What is cross-talk and what can be done about it?  
Cross-talk refers to interference between adjacent slices in MR imaging. Cross-talk arises because the slice profiles generated by RF- pulses are not perfectly rectangular. 
The simplest solution is to introduce small gaps on the order of 10-20% between slices so that the "tails" of the slice profiles do not overlap. If gapless imaging is desired, then two separate interleaved acquisitions may be employed.
Slice interleaving to allow contiguous slices without cross-talk. Odd-numbered slices (with 100% gaps) are obtained in one acquisition, followed by a second acquisition of even-numbered slices.
Effect of cross-talk on image contrast. On left is a SE 2000/20 image with 50% gap showing expected spin-density contrast. On right the same sequence with 0% gap demonstrating impaired contrast.
The major MR vendors use slightly different terminologies to refer to this interslice spacing. GE, Philips, and Canon use the generic term "gap" that is prescribed in mm, while Fujifilm uses the term "slice interval". Siemens specifies the slice gap indirectly, calling it the "distance factor". Siemens' "distance factor" is specified as a percentage of slice thickness rather than actual distance. So a distance factor of 20% applied to a group of 5-mm-thick slices means there is a 1-mm gap between them.
