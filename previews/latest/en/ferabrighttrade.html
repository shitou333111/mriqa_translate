Ferabright™

Is the newly approved contrast agent Ferabright™ the same as Feraheme® (ferumoxytol)? 
Except for the packaging, marketing, and clinical indications, the two drugs are nearly identical. Both are isotonic colloidal solutions of non-stoichiometric magnetite (superparamagnetic iron oxide) coated with polyglucose sorbitol carboxymethylether. Based on the product literature, the only minor difference between the two seems to be the overall colloidal particle size: for Ferabright™ the particle diameter ranges from 31 to 43 nm, while Feraheme® particles are smaller (17-31 nm). Both agents are supplied in 17 ml single-use vials containing 510 mg elemental iron (30 mg/mL). For smaller patients, Ferabright™ is also available in a 10 ml vial providing 300 mg elemental iron (30 mg/mL).
In October 2025 Ferabright™ was given FDA approval for use as an MR contrast "in adults with known or suspected neoplasms in the brain". The wording of this approval gives wide latitude for the drug's use, as most neurologic symptoms could conceivably arise from a potential neoplasm. 
Administration and use of Ferabright™ are the same as that for ferumoxytol described in the prior Q&A. In brief, to minimize the incidence of allergic reactions and hypotension, the drug must be diluted with saline and injected slowly over 15 minutes. Extreme care must be employed not to allow contrast extravasation that may result in irreversible skin staining.

Because of its long intravascular half-life (~14.5 hr) and slow uptakie into macrophages, postcontrast brain imaging to detect brain tumors should not generally be performed until the next day. Additionally, Ferabright™-enhancing lesions may demonstrate different sizes (frequently larger), intensity, and pattern of enhancement compared to Gd-enhanced studies, so care should be excerised when comparing lesions in this scenario.
