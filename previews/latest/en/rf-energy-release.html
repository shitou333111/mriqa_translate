Release of Energy

I think I now understand how an RF-pulse adds energy to the sytem, but how is that energy released?
In the prior Q&A we discussed how a 90° RF-pulse adds a tiny amount of energy to the spin system (~100 picojoules per gram of tissue). In the ideal case (uniform Bo and B1, short-duration RF-pulse with no T1 or T2 relaxation, exactly on resonance, and no spin-spin interactions) the RF-pulse does not alter the initial Boltzmann distribution of spin energies; it causes a unitary rotation of the spin system so that the absorbed energy is reversibly stored as transverse coherence with a macroscopic transverse magnetization.  This energy is potentially recoverable because the RF-pulse has performed coherent work (not heating). System entropy and temperature remain unchanged.
After RF-excitation the spin system is in a non-equilibrium, high-energy state. Its Zeeman energy is greater than thermal equilibrium allows. Without applying additional direct RF manipulation of the spin system, energy must flow outward. As per Einstein's famous paper of 1917, emission of energy from an atomic system is either spontaneous or induced (stimulated).  
Spontaneous vs Induced Emission of Energy
Spontaneous emission of energy is a radiative process involving the release of a photon and typified by phenomena such as fluorescence and phosphorescence.  Einstein showed that the probability of spontaneous emission is strongly frequency-dependent (proportional to f³).  In the visible region of the spectrum, f ≈ 10¹² Hz, and spontaneous emission is a dominant process.  In the radiofrequency range where NMR energies are found, however, f ≈ 100 MHz, and so spontaneous emission becomes extremely improbable. A nuclear spin in isolation would take millions of years to relax via radiation. Spontaneous emission is essentially nonexistent in NMR. Energy emission in NMR must be therefore be induced (stimulated).

Here is another way to think about this concept: The energy gap between the spin-up and spin-down states in NMR is really quite small by atomic emission standards — at 1.5T it is only about 2 x 10−7 eV (electron-volts). By comparison, visible light photons have energies of about 2 eV, or 10 million times higher. There is thus a considerable "advantage" for a high-energy light photon to be emitted by phosphorescence, but relatively little "motivation" for an already low energy nuclear spin to switch states spontaneously.

Induced emission of energy in NMR requires direct interaction of a nucleus with its external environment.  But not just any interaction will suffice. The interaction must involve stimulation by transverse electromagnetic fields having components near the Larmor frequency.  

​The external environment that interacts with the nuclei and absorbs nearly all of the released energy is known as the "lattice". This confusing term is a holdover from condensed matter physics research of the 1930s. It has unfortunately become entrenched in the modern NMR lexicon even though we deal primarily with liquids and solids lacking a crystal framework.

Today the term "lattice" is used primarily to refer to nearby nuclei, electrons, molecules, and their associated electromagnetic fields. In certain contexts even may also include more distant entities with which a spin system could react, such as RF-coils or foreign objects. Mechanisms by which energy is transferred from the spin system to the lattice include: molecular rotations, vibrations, translational motion, dipolar fluctuations, electric field fluctuations, and radiation damping. 
Taken together all these processes result in transformation of spin energy (stored by either transverse coherence or from Zeeman population imbalance) into the lattice as heat.  Some more modern texts may refer to the lattice as the "thermal reservoir" or "heat bath". Together these mechanism of energy transfer to the lattice are called alternatively T1-relaxation, spin-lattice relaxation, or thermal relaxation, the subject of multiple later Q&A's.
