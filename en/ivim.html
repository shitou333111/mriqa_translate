IVIM Imaging

What is intravoxel incoherent motion (IVIM)? Is this the same as diffusion? 
Denis Le Bihan, father of modern diffusion imaging, coined the term intravoxel incoherent motion (IVIM) in the 1980s to refer to the microscopic translation of water molecules within a voxel during an MR experiment. When gradients are applied during evolution of the MR signal, IVIM causes spin dephasing and signal loss.
If the gradients are relatively strong, IVIM-induced signal losses are primarily due to diffusion — the Brownian motion of water molecules in and around cells. When weaker gradients are used, however, a second IVIM mechanism also contributes to signal loss — microcirculation of blood in the capillary network. Microscopic perfusion effects have been largely ignored in the 25 years since Le Bihan's seminal papers, but have recently enjoyed a renaissance in what is now known as IVIM imaging.
As described in a prior Q&A, modern imaging systems measure MR signal intensity (S) from each voxel both with and without diffusion-sensitizing gradients. Often only two b-values are used: very low (e.g., b= 0-50 s/mm²  and high (e.g. 800-1000 s/mm²). From these two measurements it is possible to calculate the apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC), a useful but crude index based on a single exponential model expressing MR signal intensity (S) relative to baseline (So) as  

S = Soe−b•ADC       or       ln(S) = ln(So) − b•ADC

Under this model, a semilogarithmic plot of signal as ln (Signal) vs b should be a straight line with slope = −ADC. (Solid red line in graph below).

Deviation of the expected MR signal in DWI from a mono-exponential model. At low b-values IVIM effects due to microscopic perfusion must be considered (purple), while at high b-values kurtosis effects must be considered (green).
When careful signal measurements are made using a large number of different b-values, however, considerable departures from this simplified mono-exponential model are observed. At low b-values (i.e., ≤ 300 s/mm²) the signal is drops off more quickly from So than expected due to increased IVIM losses from microscopic perfusion. At larger b-values (≥ 1000−1500 s/mm²), signal is often greater than expected from the crude ADC line, an effect due to the non-Gaussian shape (kurtosis) of the diffusion probability distribution further discussed in the next Q&A.
To explaing this variance at low and intermediate b-values, Le Bihan proposed a bi-exponential model taking into account both diffusion and perfusion effects.  Here he distinguished and named two diffusion coefficients, D ("pure" diffusion) and D* ("pseudo"-diffusion), the former reflecting diffusion in the extravascular space and the latter reflecting dephasing due to perfusion in semi-randomly organized capillaries. Vascular density was described by a dimensionless perfusion fraction (f), the percent of a voxel volume occupied by capillaries. Conversely (1−f) reflected the extravascular space where only pure diffusion took place. Depending on the steepness of the initial portion of the curve, (which in turn depends on capillary density and perfusion), D* may be 5-10x greater than pure D.effects, allowing the biexponential model to be simplified to 

S/So = fe−bD* + (1−f)e−bD

By acquiring DWI images at multiple b-values and fitting the data to above equation, it is possible to estimate f, D, and D* and create maps for each. Typically between 6 and 10 data sets are obtained with b-values ranging between 0 and 1000 s/mm², with at least half the measurements performed at less than 250 s/mm².

However, considerable errors occur in the estimation of D*, which I personally feel are unreliable, varying too much among centers and studies. Not only D*, but all IVIM parameter estimates are affected by motion, noise, and secretory physiologic processes (such as glandular and renal tubular flows) that cannot easily be distinguished from capillary perfusion. I thus remain a little skeptical for now as to what extent IVIM imaging will find a major role in our future imaging armamentarium.
