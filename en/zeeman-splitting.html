Zeeman Splitting 

Why do spin-up and spin-down states have different energies?
A commonly used diagram to illustrate the two pure spin states
of a proton (represented as a tiny bar magnets). But don't take
this picture too seriously; see warning below
In a previous Q&A we introduced the concept of the two spin states of the ¹H nucleus, commonly denoted as |+½⟩ and |-½⟩ or  |↑⟩ and |↓⟩. We also showed how the separation and measurement of such states could be obtained by passing the spins through a magnetic field (Bo) as Stern and Gerlach did in 1922. Because the spins tend to sort themselves into orientations either generally aligned with or opposed to the external field, the two spin states are often referred to as "spin-up" or "parallel" and "spin-down" or "anti-parallel".  
Warning: Although we may sometimes be able to measure discrete properties (like energy levels or angular momentum) corresponding to "pure" (eigen) states of a spin system, it is incorrect to infer that spins physically reside exclusively in only one of these states as most exist in a mixture. 
​Magnetic Potential Energy: Classical Analysis
An intuitive model of Zeeman splitting with nuclear spin as a flipping bar magnet embedded in a large external (N-S) field.
In the Stern–Gerlach apparatus, spin-up and spin-down particles are spatially separated by the force exerted on their magnetic moments in a non-uniform magnetic field. This force arises from the Zeeman interaction, which produces an energy splitting between spin states in a magnetic field. The associated energy splitting is known as the (nuclear) Zeeman effect, named after Dutch physicist Pieter Zeeman, who in 1896 observed the field-dependent splitting of optical spectral lines of a sodium flame.

The animated image above gives some insight into why the spin-up and spin-down states are separated in energy. In the spin-down state, the red pole of the small magnet faces downward toward the red pole of the large magnet. To achieve this arrangement requires the input of energy to overcome the natural repulsive force of the like magnetic poles. The system is in a less stable, higher energy configuration. Conversely, in the spin-up state, opposite poles of the small and large magnets are adjacent and attracive, resulting in a lower magnetic potential system energy.  ​
These concepts can be made more quantitative by using classic electromagnetic theory, where we model the nuclear spin as a magnetic dipole (µ). ​The torque and energy relationships of µ in relation to Bo were carefully laid out in a prior Q&A. To summarize these results: 
  • The magnetic potential energy (E) possessed by the dipole (µ) is given by the vector dot product:    E = − µ • Bo  =  −µ Bo cos (θ), where θ is the angle between µ and Bo. 
  • When µ is aligned parallel to Bo, θ = 0​° and cos (θ) = 1,  So the system energy is at its lowest level, having a negative value (= −µBo).  
  • When µ and Bo ​ are anti-parallel, ​​θ = 180​°  and cos (θ) = −1. The two minus signs cancel and the system energy is now positive (+ µBo) and at its highest possible level.
  • The energy differential (ΔE) between parallel and anti-parallel configurations of µ relative to Bo is therefore ΔE  =  µBo − (−µBo)  =  +2 µBo 
  • This result parallels Zeeman's observation that the degree of splitting is directly proportional to magnetic field strength (Bo).
  • For a spin-½ nucleus with magnetic moment μ = γℏ/2, this becomes ΔE  = 2µBo = γℏBo​
Extension to Quantum Mechanics 
Extending this classical dipole analogy into the quantum realm requires postulating that the magnetic moment of a spin (µ) is directly related to its angular momentum, I.   The relationship is expressly defined as 
​µ = γ I 
where γ is a constant called the gyromagnetic ratio.  The gyromagnetic ratio is experimentally determined and is different for each spinning particle. For example, the value of γ is 42.58 MHz/T for the ¹H nucleus, but −​32.43 MHz/T for ³He. The linear relation between spin and angular momentum is derived from the classical electromagnetic physics of moving charges and preserved in quantum mechanics because magnetic dipole moments and angular momentum share the same rotational transformation properties. 

In quantum mechanics, operators are mathematical entities used to represent physical observables such as spin angular momentum (I). One of the most important of these is the Hamiltonian Operator (ℋ) . The system’s Hamiltonian determines its quantized energy levels through an eigenvalue equation. For simple systems, such as a single spin-½ nucleus in a static magnetic field Bo aligned along the z-axis, the Hamiltonian is Hz = −γℏBoIz. Solving the corresponding eigenvalue problem yields two observable energy levels, [−½​γℏBo] and [+½​γℏBo], whose whose separation defines the Zeeman splitting:

ΔΕ = γ ℏ​ Bo

Here we have used the form of the gyromagnetic ratio (γ) having units of angular rotation divided by field strength (rad/T). This equation introduces another parameter (ℏ) known as the reduced Planck's constant, a number that frequently arises in quantum mechanical calculations reflecting the granularity of the subatomic world. Its measured value is approximately 1.05457 x 10-34 Joule-sec. Unlike the gyromagnetic ratio (γ) that has a different value for every nucleus, ​the reduced Planck constant (ℏ) is universal for all nuclei.

The reduced Planck's constant (ℏ) is related to the more commonly recognized (non-reduced) Planck's constant (h) by the equation ℏ = h/(2π). The 2π factor converts linear frequency (cycles per second, Hz) to angular frequency (radians per second, rad/s), the latter of which is more widely used for describing rotational and wave phenomena in quantum mechanics.

In words, the Zeeman splitting realtion above states that the energy gap (ΔE) along the magnetic field direction between spin-up |+½⟩ and spin-down |−½⟩ states is directly proportional to field strength (Bo). This has important ramifications in NMR in that higher magnetic fields lead to increased the separation of energy levels producing stronger signals and the ability to separate spectral lines among chemical groups. In conjunction with Planck's Law (discussed below), the Zeeman relation also leads directly to one of the fundamental concepts underlying NMR, the famous Larmor equation.

German physicist Max Planck was one of the founding fathers of quantum mechanics. In the early 1900s he proposed that energy exchange by atomic systems could only occur in discrete packets or quanta. This concept emerged as an experimentally motivated assumption to explain black body radiation, not something derived from deeper classical laws of physics. It has subsequently been validated by multiple independent experiments, and is now treated as a fundamental postulate embedded in the structure of quantum theory itself.

Planck discovered that energy exchange (ΔE) was mediated by quanta at a specific frequency, denoted by the Greek letter "nu" ν or fo = ωo/2π. This relationship is expressed in the famous Planck's equation (now taught in high school physics classes and a building block of modern quantum mechanics):

ΔE  =  h ​ν  =  h fo  =  h (ωo/2π)  =  (h/2π) ωo  =  ℏ ωo
The Planck and Zeeman equations may be combined, producing an interesting and important result that will look familiar to those with prior exposure to MRI:
ΔE  =  ℏ ωo  =  γ ℏ Bo
or
ωo   =  γ Bo
This is the famous Larmor equation, showing that NMR resonance frequency (ωo) is simply the gyromagnetic ratio (γ) times the magnetic field strength (Bo). The fact that Planck's constant (h) disappears from the solution implies that a non-quantum explanation of NMR using classical physics is both possible and reasonable. 
Several technical terms containing Zeeman's name have made it into the modern NMR lexicon (although not described specifically by him), including Zeeman splitting, Zeeman level, Zeeman energy, and Zeeman reservoir.  These are defined and explained for interested students in the Advanced Discussion below.

Based on his experiments in 1896, Dutch physicist Pieter Zeeman concluded that optical spectral line splitting of a sodium flame is proportional to the applied magnetic field strength. This proportionality is the central empirical result now called the Zeeman effect. Although his findings were later connected to spin-½ systems and NMR, Zeeman interpreted his observations in classical terms — that electrons in atoms behave like tiny oscillating charges whose motion is modified by a magnetic field. It should be remembered that in 1896 quantum mechanics did not exist, the concepts of electron and nuclear spins were unknown, and even atomic energy levels were not fully accepted. The modern view of the Zeeman effect as arising from quantized angular momentum states was developed decades later along with the maturation of quantum mechanics.

In addition to the Zeeman effect splitting, Pieter Zeeman’s name has been attached to a number of related concepts in modern NMR theory:

Zeeman level is one of the discrete quantized energy states of a spin or atom in a magnetic field created by Zeeman splitting. A Zeeman level is thus a single allowed energy level corresponding to specific allowed projection of angular momentum along the field direction. In NMR, spins occupy Zeeman levels and RF radiation induces transitions between them.

Zeeman energy refers to the energy of interaction between a magnetic moment and an external magnetic field. Every Zeeman level has a Zeeman energy. Since the main magnetic field is typically defined to lie along the z-axis, the applicable Hamiltonian (a quantum mechanical operator associated with energy measurements) is Hz = − μ • Bo = − γℏBoIz (for a spin-½ nucleus along z). Each allowed eigenstate has a Zeeman energy Em = − γℏBo, where m = ±½.

Zeeman splitting is the energy gap between Zeeman levels created by the applied field, often denoted by ΔE. For a spin-½ nucleus, ΔE = E+½ − E−½ = γℏBo. When no external magnetic field is present, the Zeeman splitting is zero.

Zeeman reservoir is the store of energy contained in the spin populations aligned with the static magnetic field. In other words, it is the thermodynamic subsystem associated with the Zeeman Hamiltonian (Hz), associated with initial spin population imbalance and net longitudinal magnetization (Mz).

An RF-pulse mainly redistributes energy inside the spin system. An ideal short pulse does not change Zeeman populations, but converts Zeeman order into transverse coherence. The Zeeman reservoir is said to be “emptied” into transverse coherence, but not into the surrounding lattice. Subsequent T1 relaxation mechanisms (discussed in later Q&As) return the system to thermal equilibrium by re-establishing the Zeeman population imbalance and restoring Mz. T1 is thus a measure of how rapidly the Zeeman reservoir is "refilled" and re-equilibrated with the lattice.

Students sometimes find the following analogy to be helpful: Think of the Zeeman reservoir as a battery that stores magnetic alignment. When charged there is a large Mz, but a small Mz when discharged. The RF-pulse drains the battery converting it into a signal, and T1 effects recharge the battery with fresh spins from the lattice.


Difference between the Boltzmann distribution and Zeeman Order

The Boltzmann distribution describes how individual spins are distributed among Zeeman levels at equilibrium. It is a probability rule for individual spins. It describes “What fraction of spins would be measured as spin-up (N+/N) vs spin-down (N−/N)?”

In most NMR scenarios (where Zeeman energies are defined longitudinally along the z-axis), Zeeman order refers to this same population imbalance dictated by Boltzmann statistics. If the populations are unequal (N+ ≠ N−) the system has Zeeman order. The term “order” is used because the distribution is less random (has smaller entropy) than it could be. If the populations were equal (N+ = N−) this would be a state of maximum entropy and no Zeeman order. A system lacking Zeeman order is said to have an “empty” Zeeman reservoir. Zeeman order is thus a thermodynamic structure inside the spin system. Non-NMR examples of energy-imposed statistical order include magnetization in a ferromagnetic, barometric imbalance in a gravitational field, and polarization of an electric field.
