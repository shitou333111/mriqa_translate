Artficial Intelligence in Cardiac MRI

How are AI methods changing and improving cardiovascular MRI both now and in the future?
LIke many other aspects of imaging, the rapid growth of artificial intelligence methods has already positively impacted cardiovascular MRI in terms of speed, efficiency, and quality. Its effects are far-ranging, from order entry and scheduling to reporting and prognosis. An entire section of MRIQuestions.com is devoted to a more detailed discussion of AI methods in general. In this article we will focus primailry on acquisition and reconstruction, quality, and quantification as it applies to cardiac MRI.

Acquisition and Reconstruction
One of the greatest challenges of CMR is the need to rapidly acquire multiple images throughout the cardiac cycle at different image locations.  This means that k-space must be sampled incompletely to keep image acquistion times within reasonable limits. Futhermore, such undersampling of k-space data leads to increased artifacts and either decreased spatial- or temporal-resolution or both. Fortunately, deep learning (DL) methods are able to address all these limitations.
Deep learning techniques can be used at several locations along the acquisition-reconstruction pipeline.
The AI process may begin as early as initial data acquisition.  For example, it may be employed to stop image acquisition once a certain threshold has been reached or to recognize corrupted data samples and repeat them.  At the next level, deep learning can improve channel combination, using information about individual coil sensitivities and noise maps. DL may also be applied to fully combined complex data during the k-space reconstruction process. The desired signal-to-noise level from such DL-enhanced hybrid reconstructions can be adjusted by a radiologist-selectable tuning parameter (low/medium/high). The radiologist may also choose parameters that balance spatial resolution, noise, and imaging time.
Although generic techniques have been developed, for the most part AI-based reconstruction methods are vendor-dependent and highly proprietary. Examples include Siemens' Deep Resolve, GE's Air™Recon DL, and Philips' SmartSpeed.

Post-Processing, Segmentation, and Radiomics 
Further image improvement, such as increased spatial resolution or decreased noise may also be achieved after image reconstruction has been completed. Most are based on convolutional neural networks, generative adverserial networks, and/or transformer neural networks, often in a "U-configuration".  
Automated image segmentation and quantitative measurements can also be automatically performed. These methods permit considerable time savings for the MR technologist and often more consistent and reproducable results.
The myocardium is segmented either manually or automatically using DL algorithms. Feature extraction can then be performed. In cardiac MRI extraction is mostly intensity (histogram) based, supplemented by texture features and filter decomposition by methods such as the wavelet transform. After extracting multiple features, high-level statistical modelling is applied to perform classification tasks or make predictions in a given dataset.
