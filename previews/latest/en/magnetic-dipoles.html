Magnetic Moments and Dipoles

What is a magnetic dipole moment?   
Magnetic field due to a hypothetical dipole

​The magnetic moment (µ) is a vector quantity used to measure the tendency of an object to interact with an external magnetic field. In NMR, the object of interest is typically a molecule, atom, nucleus, or subatomic particle. The object's intrinsic magnetic properties are often visualized as emanating from a tiny bar magnet with north and south poles (the "dipoles"), and is therefore also called the magnetic dipole moment. 
Magnetic dipole modelled as a current loop
An alternative and more quantitatively useful definition of the magnetic moment is to model it as arising from a tiny current (i) traveling around the edge of a loop of cross sectional area (A).  The magnetic dipole moment (µ) is a vector defined as µ = i A whose direction is perpendicular to A and determined by the right-hand rule. 
The magnetic dipole moment (µ) is often used as an analog of nuclear (or electron) spin. Although not quantum mechanical by definition, µ nevertheless exhibits many NMR-like features in its interaction with an external magnetic field (Bo) — including precession, torque, and potential energy levels  — all analyzable using the laws of classical electromagnetism.
Moreover, the concept of µ is not restricted to the modeling of atomic-sized particles and can be applied to much larger objects and collections of objects.  A compass needle, MR scanner, and even the earth itself might be considered giant dipoles. What we will later define as the net magnetization (M) of a system of spins can be modeled as the vector sum of multiple individual dipole moments (µi). When the field lines of two magnetic moments cross, a dipole-dipole interaction occurs. This is an important mechanism for magnetic relaxation between two protons or between a proton and an electron in NMR about which much more will be addressed in later Q&A's.
Magnetic Torque and Energy

​Our experience in the macroscopic world provides valuable insight into forces, torques, and energy levels of magnetic dipole moments. Let us first consider the needle of hand-held compass as it seeks to align itself with the earth's magnetic field (Bo). In this analogy, the compass needle itself represents the dipole moment (µ).

​As shown in the diagram right, the natural state of an unperturbed compass needle is to point at the (magnetic) north pole. At rest parallel to Bo the needle is in stationary equilibrium and at its lowest energy level. To rotate the needle away from its initial alignment would require a "push"— an input of external energy. So displaced, the system is now in a higher energy state, the input work being stored as magnetic potential energy. 
​For a classical magnetic dipole such as a compass needle, alignment with the external field represents a stable minimum of mechanical potential energy. Rotating the needle requires external work, which is stored as magnetic potential energy. This behavior differs fundamentally from that of nuclear spin ensembles in NMR, where RF excitation produces coherent population rearrangement rather than stored mechanical energy.
Like the physically rotated compass needle (freed from its pushing finger), a displaced magnetic moment (µ) will seek to realign with the external field (Bo), experiencing a twisting force known as torque (τ). Torque has both magnitude and direction, being a vector defined mathematically by the cross product;   τ = µ x Bo.  The direction of τ is along the thumb of the right hand with the fingers curling from µ to Bo. ||τ || = ||µ|| ||Bo​|| sin(θ​), where θ is the angle between µ and Bo. Since the units of µ are (A-m²)  and Bo is measured in Tesla (N/A-m), the units of this magnetic torque are therefore N-m.

The magnetic potential energy (E) possessed by the dipole (µ) is given by the vector dot product by the formula:    E = − µ • Bo  =  −µ Bo cos(θ​), where θ is the angle between µ and Bo. Note that in contrast to torque, the energy depends on the cosine of the angle between the vectors, not the sine.
When µ and Bo ​ are aligned, cos(0​°) = 1 so the system energy is a negative number (= −µBo).  When µ and Bo ​ opposed, cos(180​°) = −1 the system energy is now positive (+ µBo) and at its highest possible level. The energy input (ΔE) required to invert µ from its initial parallel to opposed orientation to Bo is therefore  µBo − (−µBo) = 2 µBo.

If you are a fan of CBS' The Big Bang Theory, you may remember an episode in Season 2 where Sheldon decides to spend the summer at the north pole searching for the elusive "Magnetic Monopole", hoping such a discovery would win him the Nobel Prize.

This is not fiction at all, as scientists for the last 80 years have been seeking a solitary magnetic particle that is the equivalent of a solitary electric charge. If they exist, magnetic monopoles must be extremely rare, and to date, no one has conclusively found one. But the search continues...
