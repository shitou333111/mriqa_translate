Iron-based Contrast Agents

How are iron-based contrast agents used for MR imaging?
Coexistent with the success of gadolinium-based contrast agents in the 1980's, companies like Advanced Magnetics (later renamed AMAG and now a subsidiary of the Covis Pharma) began to research and market MR contrast agents based on iron oxides. Their original products Feridex (for liver imaging) and GastroMark (a GI contrast) were followed by Combidex (for lymph node use) and ferumoxytol for intravascular use. Interestingly, AMAG decided not to market ferumoxytol as a contrast agent, but rather as Feraheme, a treatment for iron-deficiency anemia. The imaging value of ferumoxytol was still recognized by the MR community and a number of sites began using Feraheme "off-label" as a contrast agent, In late 2025, ferumoxytol was FDA approved as a contrast agent for brain tumors and marketed as Ferabright. 

Iron atoms and ions have strong magnetic moments due to 4 or 5 unpaired electrons in their 3d shells. Iron-based contrast agents typically contain a core of magnetite (Fe3O4) surrounded by a thick polysaccharide coating.The total number of iron atoms within each core depends on the particle size and composition. Smaller particles like ferumoxytol have a single nanocrystal core with diameter of about 5-7 nm containing about 2000 magnetite (Fe3O4) units. Larger particles like Feridex contain tens to hundreds of iron oxide cores agregated together inside the particle’s dextran shell.

Tranmission electron micrograph of single core magnetite nanoparticles. Core size is about 5 nm and overal particle size (that includes the capsule) is about 40 nm.
When the core size reaches about 3-5 nm, the unpaired electron spins become entwined by a quantum mechancial force known as exchange interaction (coupling). The core then acts as a single magnetic domain and the particle exhibits superparamagnetism when placed in an exterrnal magnetic field. Such particles are commonly referred to as  superparamagnetic particles of iron oxide (SPIOs) .
SPIOs are often subdivided into several groups depending on their total hydrodynamic diameter (including their coatings and hydration layer). Note that many formulations have a range of particle sizes and could belong to more than one group. Although definitions and specific size criteria may very, here is a reasonable classification of SPIOs from smallest to largest:
  • Extremely Small Particles of Iron Oxide (ESPIOs): less than 5-10 nm in diameter
  • Very Small Superparamagnetic Particles of Iron Oxide (VSPIOs): 10-25 nm in diameter.
  • Ultra-Small Superparamagnetic Particles of Iron Oxide (USPIOs): 25–50 nm in diameter, the smaller ones often often grouped with VSPIO due to similar features.
  • Small Superparamagnetic Particles of Iron Oxide (SSPIOs): 50–500 nm in diameter
  • Micro-sized Particles of Iron Oxide (MPIO): from about 500–5000 nm (0.5–5.0 μm) in diameter.
Compared to larger particles, ESPIOs have a high surface-area-to-volume ratio with two implications. First, their relatively large surface areas facilitate close interactions of surrounding water molecules, making them primarily useful as T1-relaxation agents. Secondly, because of spin canting effects (non-collinear alignment of surface spins) and Néel relaxation dynamics (spontaneous, fast flipping of the magnetic momenta), their net magnetization is dramatically reduced compared to larger particles.
Thus ESPIO's behave in more "paramagnetic-like" rather than superparamagnetic fashion.

At the other extreme, MPIOs are giant conglomerate particles with extremely high iron content compared to smaller SPIOs. A typical MPIO particle contains approximately 1 picogram (pg) of iron per particle. They are hence very efficient at T2* relaxation, and are being used in experimental and molecular imaging for cellular tracking.
