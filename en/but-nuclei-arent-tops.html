Nuclear Precession

But nuclei aren't exactly tops! So why are they precessing?
You are quite correct. Nuclear spins in a magnetic field behave similarly to tops and gyroscopes, but not exactly. For example, a top may achieve any angular position in relation to the gravitational field (although the energies and initial conditions will be different for different orientations). But a nuclear spin can only achieve a finite set of orientations with respect to the applied field because its allowed directions are restricted (quantized).
Let's begin with a few basic concepts about nuclear spin and its precession. (Most of these ideas can be found in this prior Q&A and with minor modifications also apply to electrons). But for now we will keep things simple by relating everything to a single spin-½ ¹H proton. 
A fundamental postulate in quantum mechanics is that that the magnetic moment of a spin (µ) is directly proportional to its angular momentum (I).  This relationship is expressly defined by the equation ​µ = γ I, where γ is the gyromagnetic ratio (an empirically measured constant unique for each type of nucleus.) Because µ and I are co-linear, the two will faithfully follow each other's precession and it is only necessary to display one or the other in vector diagrams.  
The diagram (right) shows a magnetic dipole modeled as a small current loop having spin angular momentum (I). The interaction with Bo produces twisting forces (F) on the current loop that create a torque (τ) attempting to align I with Bo. The torque is a vector perpendicular to both I and Bo and points out of the page according to the right-hand rule.
If I were a compass needle or simple bar magnet, the generated torque would merely cause I to oscillate in a plane back and forth in the direction of Bo.  However, since I possesses angular momentum, the result is precession.
The reason for this behavior may not be immediately obvious. Although the torque acts to align I with Bo, angular momentum, being what it is (momentum!), cannot change its direction instantaneously.

Nuclear precession "cone"
Because the torque is always perpendicular to I and Bo, the change in angular momentum (dI) must also be perpendicular to both of them. Accordingly the tip of I must move in a plane perpendicular to Bo.  And because the magnitude of I is fixed (due to quantum mechanical restrictions), the result is a circular precession of I around the Bo direction resembling a cone.

So “precession frequency of a spin in a static field” and “Larmor frequency” are not two different things that just happen to match. The Larmor frequency is defined as the solution of the torque equation for a magnetic moment in a static field, i.e., the precession rate of the spin vector itself. ​Precession occurs at the Larmor frequency because the same coupling constant (γ) that sets the Zeeman energy splitting also determines the torque, and angular momentum under torque can only evolve by precessing at that rate.
