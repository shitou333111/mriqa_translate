CW v Pulsed NMR

What is the difference between Continuous Wave (CW) and Pulsed RF excitation?
So far we have established that radiofrequency (RF) excitation near the Larmor frequency is an essential requirement for NMR to occur. As described in later Q&As, RF is transmitted as electromagnetic radiation by specialized coils located near the patient (or sample). RF energy delivery may be in the form of continuous wave (CW) transmission or by one or more short bursts (known as "RF pulses"). 
Continuous Wave (CW) NMR techniques were the earliest to be developed and widely used throughout the 1950's and 1960's. By the mid-1960's, however, pulsed methods had become widespread and by the 1980s had nearly completely replaced CW techniques. Limitations and disadvantages of CW NMR that led to its decline included low signal-to-noise, long acquisition times, inability to encode multiple frequencies, sample heating, and inability to easily manipulate relaxation and coherence pathways. 

​IIn a typical CW experiment, RF excitation is applied continuously for times long compared to the spin–lattice relaxation time (T1), typically seconds to minutes. A steady-state driven system is established in which the RF field continuously induces transitions between the Zeeman states. Stimulated absorption (|α⟩→|β⟩) and stimulated emission (|β⟩→|α⟩) occur at equal RF-induced rates, while spin–lattice relaxation simultaneously exchanges energy between the spin system and its environment (“the lattice”). As the RF field repeatedly drives transitions in both directions, the population difference between the Zeeman levels is progressively reduced. If the RF excitation is sufficiently strong and maintained long enough, the populations approach equality, the longitudinal magnetization decreases, and the system reaches partial (or even complete) magnetic saturation.
​Pulsed NMR  is quite different in its methods and intended effects on the spin system. RF pulses in MRI are typically less than a few milliseconds in length (even as short as μs in laboratory NMR spectrometers) and therefore very short compared with the spin–lattice (T1) and spin–spin (T2) relaxation times. Relaxation processes can thus be largely neglected during their application. In the ideal case no saturation occurs because essentially no irreversible energy exchange takes place between the spins and their external environment (“the lattice”) during the pulse. Instead, the RF field coherently mixes the Zeeman states, producing a unitary rotation of the spin system as a whole. The populations of the energy levels are not irreversibly redistributed; rather, the initial Boltzmann population imbalance (Zeeman order) is rotated into transverse spin coherence.
