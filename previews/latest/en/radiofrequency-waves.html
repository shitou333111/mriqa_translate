What is a radiofrequency (RF) wave?
Electric (E) and magnetic (B) fields can be either static or dynamic. Static behavior is exemplified by one's hair standing on end in dry weather or a magnet sticking to a refrigerator door. In the dynamic mode, E and B fields are oscillating and linked. A changing electric field will induce a changing magnetic field and vice-versa. This coupling leads in free space to self-propagating electromagnetic waves in which the electric and magnetic fields are perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation, oscillating in phase and transporting energy and momentum.
A propagating planar electromagnetic wave showing the perpendicular directions of the simultaneously oscillating electric (E) and magnetic (B) fields

Electromagnetic waves span a wide spectrum from high to low energy and frequency. (from NASA.gov)
The electromagnetic waves associated with NMR come from the lower frequency/energy part of the spectrum. This segment is often referred to as the radiofrequency (RF) region and is used for radio, television, aviation, and satellite communications. The table (right) shows where the operating frequencies of various strength MRI scanners fit into the RF spectrum. Without RF-shielding, these commercial applications could impede accurate measurement of the MR signal.
