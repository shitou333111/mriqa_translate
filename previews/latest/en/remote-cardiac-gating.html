Remote/Contactless Cardiac Gating

How can cardiac gating be performed without sensors affixed to the patient?  
Siemens Beat Sensor (orange dot) affixed to chest receiver array
Over the past decade methods for contactless gating have been developed that do not require dedicated skin electrodes, belts, or attached sensors. At present this system is proprietary to Siemens and can be used either for cardiac gating (called Beat Sensor​™) or  respiratory gating (called BioMatrix™).  The latter will be discussed in a separate Q&A. 
Pulse-tone in k-space is offset from the main imaging frequency band to allow its extraction.
(From Ludwig under CC-BY-4.0)
Contactless gating employs a continuous RF-signal known as the pilot tone (PT) separate from the main RF used for imaging. The signal amplitude of the pilot tone is modulated due to differences in coil loading impedance during heart or chest motion. 

The PT for cardiac gating is transmitted into the patient's chest through a dedicated transmitter and coil located on the anterior thorax array. Its frequency is offset by about +100 kHz from the system (Larmor) frequency so that it can be easily separated from the main imaging data in the k-space receive coil chain. With calibration and modeling, the PT signal can be converted to  cardiac phase. 
An approximately 1 minute calibration scan must be performed before using the Beat Sensor, but then it is ready to go. Triggering is most reliable in the acceleration phase of early mid-systole. Note that there is approximately a 200 ms delay between the R-wave and peak PT signal.
