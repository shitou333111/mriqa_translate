MR Fingerprinting


What is MR Fingerprinting (MRF)? 
MR fingerprinting (MRF) is a quantitative approach to MRI diagnosis based on the rapid and simultaneous measurement of multiple tissue properties, such as T1 and T2 relaxation times. Instead of traditional sequences, MRF rapidly acquires a series of several hundred images with semi-randomly varying parameters like TR, TE, and flip angle and then compares the signal evolution of each pixel to a pre-built database (or "dictionary") to generate a unique "fingerprint" for each tissue. The potential (not yet fully realized) is to produce more objective and quantitative differentiation between healthy and diseased tissue than that based purely on visual analysis.  
MRF is still in evolution and no commercially distributed products are available (although major vendors like Siemens offer prototype sequences and development kits), Although no universally agreed-upon single MRF method is available, most share several common features. 
Image Acquisition
  • Core pulse sequence is usually a multislice-2D SSFP-GRE acquiring several hundred images in each anatomic partition simultaneously. For 3D acquisitions, EPI is often used.  
  • The core sequence is punctuated by semi-random variation of TR values (10-100 ms); low (~1°) to moderate (~70°) flip angles (arranged in characteristic “humps” to optimize image contrast); 1-2 second pauses (TR "spikes"); intermittent application of dephasing gradients, preparation, and inversion pulses; TE's are usually kept constant and short (2-5 ms), but sometimes varied 
  • Non-Cartesian k-space trajectories (like spiral, radial, or stack-of-stars) are usually employed to allow fast, undersampled acquisitions.
  • Total scan time of seconds (for a single 2D slice) up to 5-10 min for multislice 2D and 3D.
  • This semi-randomized approach creates a unique, time-resolved signal evolution, or "fingerprint," for each voxel based on its specific physical properties. 
The MRF "Dictionary"
  • The MRF "dictionary" is a large database of signal time courses (fingerprints) based on combinations of tissue parameters (e.g. T1, T2), pulse sequence variations, and k-space trajectories using Bloch equation simulations.
  • For each set of parameters, the resulting signal time course is stored as a vector in the dictionary, forming a large multidimensional lookup table.
Pattern Matching
  • After acquiring the MRF images, the time-dependent signal evolution is extracted voxel-wise.
  • Each voxel’s measured signal is then compared to every entry in the dictionary. A similarity metric such as the inner product or normalized correlation is used to find the best matching dictionary element. This is a lengthy process that requires special computing resources and may take hours for large data sets.
  • To speed up matching, strategies like averaging or filtering methods, grouping dictionary entries, principal component analysis, tree-based searches, or deep learning methods are now being used.
  • A quantitative parameter map for each entire image may be created using the best match assigned to each voxel.

Although MR Fingerprinting (MRF) offers significant advantages for multiparametric quantitative imaging, it remains primarily a research technique facing several challenges limiting its widespread clinical implementation. Key problems include susceptibility to motion artifacts, limited spatial resolution (due to undersampling acquisition), partial volume effects (in voxels at tissue boundaries), demanding computational requirements (dictionary size and processing time), and lack of clinical validation.
