"Nutation" of M by RF-excitation

How does RF tip M?
The term nutation commonly refers to the wobbling behavior of a top or gyroscope, but has a somewhat different and restricted meaning in the context of NMR.  Specifically, nutation in NMR refers to the RF-induced rotation of the net magnetization (M) out of its initial alignment with Bo. As described in a prior Q&A this rotation of M requires application of a radiofrequency field (B1) applied perpendicular to Bo with components near the Larmor frequency.
Perhaps the most straightforward explanation for nutation can be illustrated using principles of classical electromagnetism in both the laboratory (stationary) and rotating frames of reference. 
Nutation of M in the Laboratory Frame
When viewed from the external stationary (laboratory) frame of reference, the motion of M and the spin system appears rather complex. The B1 field rotates in the transverse (xy)-plane at the Larmor frequency. M, initially aligned with Bo is now tipped downward and precesses around the z-axis in concert with B1.  In the laboratory frame, M follows a spiraling trajectory with increasing transverse components (Mxy) and decreasing longitudinal components (Mz). After a period of excitation M makes an angle (α°) with the z-axis.  This is called the flip (or tip) angle.
Nutation of M in the Rotating Frame
In a frame of reference rotating at the Larmor frequency the motion of M is considerably simplified.
M (and its component individual spins) no longer appear to precess around the z-axis. Everything is 'frozen' in place so it is as though Bo no longer exists.  This is represented in the diagram as a fading away of the green Bo magnet pole.

Since both B1 and the frame move at the same frequency, they are now locked in a fixed relationship to the rotating frame. In the diagram we have arbitrarily placed B1 along the (new) x'-axis.  By adjusting the phase of its transmission, however, we could have locked B1 along the y'-axis or any other direction in the transverse plane.

​M has begun its nutation — a new precession around B1.  Since Bo no longer exists in this frame of reference, the only magnetic field acting on M is the now stationary B1 field. So M will precess around B1 with frequency  f1 = γ B1.  This nutation frequency (f1) is also known as the Rabi frequency.  Because B1 is typically only one-millionth the strength of Bo, the precession of M around B1 will be relatively slow (measured in Hz rather than MHz). 
The Quantum Mechanical Perspective on Nutation
The ideas embodied in the classical description above are confirmed and mirrored using the more exacting mathematics of quantum mechanics. Quantum mechanics provides s a better understanding of how individual spins evolve during the course of RF-excitation.
​Recall that only a tiny thermally polarized fraction of spins contributes to the system's net magnetization (M) and macroscopic NMR signal. Although the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle prevents us from simultaneously specifying all components of the angular momentum of an individual spin, quantum mechanics still allows us to predict and track the average (expectation-value) behavior of a large collection of spins treated as an "ensemble".
To me, one of the most surprising results from quantum mechanics is that as long as the RF field is homogeneous, the spins are non-interacting, and there is no relaxation, even continuous RF does not change their relative orientations.. An entire spin ensemble, represented by M, is merely rotated as a block by B1. In QM lingo, each spin experiences the same unitary rotation operator so that all spins undergo the same rotation angle along the same rotation axis.  As shown in the diagram below, this explains why the magnitude of M remains the same before and after RF-excitation, although undergoing a change in direction and phase. 
As long as the B1 field is left on, M can be rotated to any angle. However, neither the magnitude of M nor the relative orientation of spins changes during this rotation.
In summary, RF-excitation rotates M without changing its magnitude, but does affect both its direction and phase.  The flip angle determines the tilt of M, while the RF phase setting determines its transverse orientation.
