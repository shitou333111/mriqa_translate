Parallel (Multi-) Transmit RF

Several manufacturers now offer "multi-transmit" technology. What is it and how does it work? 
Principle of RF multi-transmit technology. Two or more coil elements are independently powered and controlled, resulting in increased speed and RF homogeneity. (Diagram courtesy of Canon).
Use of dual-transmit RF to reduce standing wave (dielectric shading) artifacts. (Courtesy Philips Medical Systems)
Multi-transmit or parallel transmission technology is one of the most exciting recent developments in MRI.  In many ways it is the natural extension of widely used parallel receiver imaging applied to optimize properties of the transmitted (B1) field.

The basic idea is relatively simple: instead of the entire RF coil transmitting a single B1 field, divide the coil into separate independently powered and controlled elements that each produce their own B1 subfields. The sum of these subfields constitutes the net B1 field experienced by the tissue.  In this manner it is possible to more carefully control the homogeneity of RF excitation as well as the distribution of magnetic and electric fields produced in tissue. 
This has the practical application of reducing standing wave (dialectric effect) shading artifacts as well as minimizing RF-energy deposition in tissues.

B1 field generated by a dual-transmit RF coil
In multi-transmit systems power is distributed to the ports of the RF transmit coil (typically a TEM resonator) using two or more independent channels.  The design of such systems requires rigorous control over timing, phase, power and amplitude of each channel, as well as various safety adaptations.  

All RF-transmitters are synchronized with sub-nanosecond precision and each channel is driven by its own solid state RF-amplifier. Each channel has its own transmit/receive (T/R) switch to allow bimodal operation if desired.  Alternatively, the resonator can be operated purely in transmit mode with separate surface coils used as receiver elements. 

An amplifier shutdown unit is required to avoid potentially hazardous increase in tissue heating should a single channel fail.  Circulators are needed to protect amplifiers against reflected or coupled power from coil elements.

Prior to imaging a calibration sequence (typically a 3D gradient echo field phase mapping technique) is required to to optimize the power, amplitude, phase and waveforms of the individual RF sources to each patient’s anatomy.  This process is called adaptive B1-shimming (described in more detail in the Advanced Discussion). Notwithstanding the time involved in this calibration, total imaging times with 2-channel RF systems may be reduced on certain high specific absorption rate (SAR) sequences by 30% or more by allowing more slices per TR.

RF (B1) Shimming

In patients RF fields propagate through inhomogeneous tissues resulting in attenuation, phase shifts, reflections and standing waves. As a result, the transmitted field becomes distorted. Instead of a perfect circle, you get skewed/stretched rotation resembling an ellipse.

To overcome these distortions, modern MR systems pre-distort the RF field in a manner called elliptical polarization to produce a better effective B1+ inside tissue. This improves efficiency (converting more RF power into usable excitation), reduces dielectric shading (especially at ≥3T), improves flip-angle uniformity, increases signal-to-noise, and reduces specific absorption rate (SAR). While these features do improve image quality at 3T, they are mandatory at 7T and above.

”True” RF-shimming requires parallel transmit capabilities. Single-channel MR scanners may perform limited RF-corrections (sometimes called “quasi-shimming”) by allowing adjustments to power balancing, amplitude attenuation, and phase delay between the rungs of quadrature coils. For example an increased phase delay may produce more of an elliptical-shaped excitation field and be more suitable for patients with elongated (rather than round) heads. Some systems offer “Preset” transmit modes that alter global loading conditions for body vs head imaging and dielectric pad optimization but are not patient-specific or dynamic.


The Future of Parallel Transmit RF

In addition to TEM resonators, a variety of interesting new coil designs exist optimized for parallel transmit technology. These include stripline and shielded loop arrays. Other advanced modifications to TEM and birdcage resonators include the use of "active rungs" which can be controlled individually by embedded electronic elements.

At present, all the major MR manufacturers offer dual transmit RF commercially and some commercial 7T systems are equipped with 8-channel parallel transmit. You can expect higher order systems in the future especially in 3T and above.

We are really just at the beginning of this new phase of MR technology. Careful control over the RF through multi-transmit techniques will open up new vistas including tailored RF's matched to individual patients, pulse sequences, and even to individual points in k-space! Three-dimensional RF stimulation is also possible.
