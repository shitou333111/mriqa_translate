Prep/Consent for CIED Patient

What special advanced preparation and planning is needed when scanning a patient with a CIED?
Performing a high-quality cardiac MR exam is a difficult endeavor, even further complicated when a patient has a conditional or non-conditional CIED.  A team approach is required between Cardiology and Radiology, who each share in several and joint responsibility for patient safety.
Planning and performing MRI exams in patients with CIEDs. (From Kim et al, under CC-BY 4.0)
Before the Scan
The patient's medical history must be thoroughly reviewed, including type of CIED and its current function/integrity.  A recent chest x-ray review is strongly suggested confirm lead and generator placement and to look for broken or abandoned leads.

​ An electrophysiologist should interrogate the device and programming to MRI mode is first performed, typically to “OVO” or “ODO” mode.  If the patient is pacemaker dependent, then asynchronous “VOO” or “DOO” modes are usually recommended.

Suggested Patient Positioning during the Scan
Martinez et al  (2019) have recently demonstrated in phantoms that a feet-first orientation of patients with left anterior throacic CIEDs may reduce lead tip heating compared to other anatomic positions. I see no reason not to do this.
After the Scan 
Although most CIEDs will continue to function normally after the scan, a few may exhibit potentially significant changes in device or lead parameters. Several pre-defined, conservative thresholds for significant changes potentially attributable to MRI include: a decrease in sensed P-wave amplitude ≥ 50%; a decrease in sensed R-wave amplitude ≥ 25%; an increase in capture threshold ≥ 0.5 volts (V); an absolute change in pacing lead impedance ≥ 50 Ω; an absolute change in high-voltage lead impedance ≥ 3 Ω; and a decrease in battery voltage ≥ 0.04 V.  If any of these are suspected, a repeat CIED interrogation by an electrophysiologist after the scan is mandatory.
