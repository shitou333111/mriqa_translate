Gradient Inserts

What are insertable gradient coils and how are they used?
 In the quest for more powerful gradients needed for high-resolution imaging, engineering considerations must address that too rapidly changing fields may cause adverse biological effects, including peripheral nerve stimulation (PNS) and cardiac arrhythmias. Additionally, as scanner bore diameters have continually increased, so has the distance between the gradient coils and the scanner isocenter.  Based on dimensional analysis, the product of the maximum gradient strength (Gmax) and maximum slew rate (SRmax) is proportional to the maximum electrical power (Pmax = Vmax x Imax) delivered to the coil divided by the fifth power of the coil diameter (d):
Although increasing gradient amplifier power is one solution, if we really want improve gradient performance dramatically, we need to reduce the coil diameter. One solution has been to employ insertable gradient coils, especially suitable for head imaging. By reducing coil diameter and PNS optimized designs (to shift to location of rapid gradient field changes away from major nerves), biological adverse effects can be minimized while gradient function is improved.​
Insertable head gradient coil for GE MAGNUS 3.0T scanner
3rd party insertable gradient coil for human head imaging (From Tesla Engineering, tesla.co.uk)
The power levels offered by such gradient inserts can be substantial. The GE MAGNUS, for example, boasts a peak gradient strength of 300 mT/m with a slew rate of 750 T/m/s.
Some companies, like Doty and Magnetica, will construct custom gradient coils for smaller human parts imaging (like knees or extremities).  At present these are specialty items confined primarily to research centers. However, insertable gradient and RF coils for animals have long been available and widely used for basic science research.
