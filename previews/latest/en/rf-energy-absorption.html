Energy Absorption

How does the spin system absorb and store energy from RF-pulses?  
Perturbation of the nuclear spin system by a radiofrequency field is required to generate an NMR signal. The applied RF field, denoted B1​, is produced by resonant coils and is oriented transverse to the main field Bo.  When B1​ oscillates near the Larmor frequency, it remains phase-matched to the spin precession and drives coherent rotation of the magnetization through reversible energy exchange with the field. Under these conditions, the system is said to be "driven on resonance".​
Note that in this Q&A (and in many that follow) we are considering the action of short-duration RF-pulses as are most commonly utilized in modern NMR/MRI equipment. (The energetics are different when continuous RF-excitation or long-duration pulses are used). In our current analysis we assume idealized conditions including: ​Bo and B1 uniformity,  on-resonance excitation, non-interacting spins, and short RF-pulses where no T1 or T2 relaxation occurs. But see Advanced Discussion for a more nuanced analysis. 

An RF pulse does add energy to the spin system, but the amount is far smaller than is often assumed. Although RF transmitter power in an MRI scanner is rated in kilowatts, nearly all of this power is dissipated through ohmic heating of the RF coil, dielectric losses in tissue, eddy-current losses, and radiative losses. Only a minute fraction is transferred directly to the nuclear spin ensemble. As a result, the energy deposited into the spins themselves may be as small as 10−10 J/g. Moreover, this energy input is largely reversible: if the spin ensemble is coherently rotated back to its original orientation by subsequent RF pulses, the injected Zeeman energy can, in principle, be recovered rather than dissipated into the lattice.

A common misconception is that an ideal 90°-pulse equalizes the initial slightly unequal Boltzmann populations along the z-axis. It does not. Although there is no measurable longitudinal magnetization after a 90°-pulse, but the fact that Mz = 0 does not mean the spin populations have equalized. It just means the population imbalance is no longer aligned with the z-axis .
What has happened is that the RF-pulse has forced the ensemble of spins to rotate as en bloc through a mechanism called coherent unitary evolution.  The population has been rotated and the spin system now resides in a coherent non-equilibrium state characterized by maximal transverse magnetization. No significant redistribution of Zeeman populations has occurred, and the system has not been thermally excited. The departure from equilibrium is encoded primarily in the off-diagonal elements of the so-called density matrix, a quantum mechanical concept that we will briefly introduce below. 
How brief RF-pulses rotate the entire spin ensemble ​
Please bear with me.  I promised not to delve too deeply into quantum mechanics, but I believe that the following description using the so-called density matrix will be more elucidating than confusing.  At least I hope so!
The density matrix (or operator), denoted by the symbol rho (ρ), is a mathematical object that contains all physically measurable information about the state of a spin system.​ In NMR you never deal with a single isolated spin and a single wavefunction (Ψ), but a huge (Avagadro's) number of spins in a statistical mixture of many quantum states. The density matrix is a probability-weighted average of quantum states — ​a "bookkeeping table" that keeps track of how many spins are in each state and how well their phases are synchronized.
The Density Matrix
For a two-level system (like spin ±½ protons), the density matrix is a 2x2 array (as shown right).  The matrix consists of two diagonal terms  (ρ↑↑ and ρ↓↓) and two off-diagonal terms (ρ↑↓ and ρ↓↑).  ​In the Zeeman basis (ie, measured along the z-axis), the diagonal terms represent the fraction of spins in each Boltzmann energy level and reflect the longitudinal magnetization (Mz). The off-diagonal elements represent the fraction of spins in phase-coherent superpositions and correspond to transverse magnetization (Mxy). 
When the system is in thermal equilibrium before an RF-pulse, the off-diagonal terms are both zero and hence there is no net transverse magnetization. The diagonal terms are each close to ½, with the original Boltzmann distribution favoring lower energy making ρ↑↑ very slightly larger than ½ by an amount ε and ρ↓↓ slightly smaller than ½ by the same amount (ε). 

Density matrix at thermal equilibrium before an RF-pulse.
Each of the ε's is a dimensionless thermal polarization parameter representing one-half the fractional population difference between the two Zeeman levels at thermal equilibrium per the high temperature limit of the Boltzmann distribution. 

Recall that this population excess at body temperatures is very small, on the order of 1 part in 10−5. Also at equilibrium a longitudinal magnetization Mz = M0 exists. For a system with n spins per unit volume, we can also write M0 = (nγℏ)ε.

A 90° RF-pulse along the x-axis applies the same unitary rotation to all spins, rotating the ensemble en bloc and creating transverse magnetization. The new density matrix is now
Density matrix after the 90°-RF pulse. The diagonal elements are now each exactly ½ so there is no net longitudinal magnetization. The small fractional excess ε has been transferred to the off-diagonal elements.
A 90°-pulse moves the tiny initial Boltzmann population imbalance from the diagonal elements into the off-diagonal elements, where it appears as transverse coherence. The presence of the imaginary numbers (±i) are just what you get from quantum mechanics, so live with it!  What this all means is that the spin system has the same eigenvalues, same entropy, and same Boltzmann polarization before and after the pulse. Only the representation (diagonal vs off-diagonal) has changed.

Density matrix after a 180°-pulse. It has the same form as the equilibrium matrix (ρeq) but its diagonal elements have been swapped.
Note that this has the same form as the matrix at equilibrium (before any RF-pulses), except that the two diagonal elements are interchanged. The amount of population imbalance (ε) is unchanged, but its direction relative to the magnetic field is reversed. During the pulse, coherence exists transiently, but it disappears when the rotation is complete. Hence the off-diagonal elements are zero. No entropy has been generated and the spin system has been placed in a metastable inverted state with the same degree of order as before.
In summary: An idealized RF pulse of short duration only rotates the spin system, converting absorbed RF energy into coherence as shown in the density matrix. After a 90°-pulse Mz is now 0 and Mxy is maximal with value Mo, no change in the original Zeeman distribution or irreversible equilibration has occurred. After a 180°-pulse Mz has been inverted (to −Mo) and Mxy is now zero.  Population equalization does not occur with ideal, short RF pulses; it arises only under conditions of sustained irradiation combined with relaxation, such as in saturation and continuous-wave excitation.

The concept in the main section above — that “RF-pulses don’t change spin populations” — is correct to first order, but only when ideal conditions are present. Such conditions include magnetic field homogeneity, excitation perfectly on resonance, validity of the rotating-wave approximation, instantaneous pulse duration with no T1 or T2 relaxation, and no internal couplings occurring during the pulse. In real life, of course, all of these conditions are usually violated to some degree. As a result, a small amount of population leakage does occur after an RF-pulse, due to three principal factors.

Off-resonance Effects

An off-resonance frequency difference (Δω) introduces small non-commuting Iz terms into the diagonal elements of density matrix (that normally contain only Ix and Iy components . The size of this effect is proportional to the square of the frequency offset to RF-frequency ratio (Δω/Ω)², where Ω = (ω1² + Δω²)½. Under normal MRI conditions these off-resonance effects are on the order of <0.1—0.5%.

Relaxation During RF-Pulse

T1 effects occur even during very short RF-pulses, which means the evolution is no longer unitary. The population change (ΔN/N) during the pulse is approximately τp/T1. For a pulse length τp ≈ 5 ms and a tissue T1 ≈ 1000 ms, this ratio is 5/1000 or approximately 0.5%.

RF-driven Dipolar Mixing

Spin-spin interactions produce tiny but nonzero changes in local populations, causing a redistribution within the Zeeman reservoir. This effect is usually <<1%.
