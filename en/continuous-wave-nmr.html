Continuous Wave (CW) NMR

What is Continuous Wave (CW) NMR?
Continuous wave (CW) excitation was used by early pioneers of NMR and remained a dominant technique for much of the mid-20th Century. Although ultimately supplanted by pulsed-RF methods, CW excitation still has some niche applications today and provides unique insights into the NMR phenomenon and its energetics. 
A chemistry laboratory CW NMR setup
The illustration (right) shows a typical CW NMR setup as might have been found in chemistry labs in the 1950s and 60s.  A sample was inserted into the space between poles of an electromagnet fitted with sweep circuitry that could incrementally increase or decrease the strength of the main magnetic field (Bo).  A low-power, perpendicularly-directed, oscillating RF-field (B1) was applied for several seconds to minutes until a steady-state was achieved. At that time the Bo field was slowly swept from lower to higher values. (In some alternative configurations the Bo field was kept constant while the B1 frequency was swept). In either case, when the B1 frequency matched the Larmor frequency of a certain nuclear species, changes in the electrical currents driving the RF system were detected corresponding to the onset of resonance. 
CW Excitation and Response
The usual goal of CW excitation is to produce a steady-state transverse magnetization that is static in the rotating frame but rotates continuously in the laboratory frame, inducing a detectable RF signal. To achieve this, the RF stimulation near the Larmor frequency must be applied applied continuously for times long compared to the spin–lattice relaxation time (T1), typically seconds to minutes. Unless full saturation is desired, the CW field is kept intentionally weak (0.01−0.1G or about 1/1000 the strength of the B1 field used for pulsed NMR).    
During CW excitation the RF field drives stimulated transitions between the Zeeman levels with equal upward and downward probabilities. These RF-driven transitions tend to equalize the spin populations, while T1​ relaxation simultaneously acts to restore the longitudinal Boltzmann distribution. When the RF transition rate becomes comparable to or greater than the relaxation rate, the longitudinal magnetization is reduced and the system becomes partially or fully saturated, leading to a diminished NMR signal.

The resulting steady-state rotation of the net magnetization (M) around the z-axis superficially resembles the spontaneous precession seen after a short-duration, high-intensity RF-pulse. But note several important differences: ​
Laboratory frame of reference shows rotation of M
about the z-axis driven by a continuous RF-field (B1)
  • The steady-state precession (rotation) of M is not the same as (energy)-free precession seen after an RF-pulse; it is a macroscopic, coherent, rotating magnetization maintained by continuous RF energy exchange.
  • Free precession after an RF pulse always occurs at the Larmor frequency; in CW NMR the transverse magnetization is phase-locked to the applied RF field and therefore rotates in the laboratory frame at the RF frequency (which may be the same or differ slightly from the  natural Larmor frequency depending on whether the RF is on- or off-resonance). 
  • After attaining the steady-state, the overall magnitude M, as well as its longitudinal (Mz) and transverse (Mxy) components remain constant.
  • After an RF-pulse the magnitude of M retains its original thermal equilibrium magnitude (Mo). In CW NMR, however, the magnitude of M is smaller than Mo due to partial saturation effects.
  • In CW NMR M attains and maintains a fixed polar angle with respect to the z-axis commonly known as the steady-state tilt angle; this homologous to but the result of a different mechanism than the generation of an RF flip angle in pulsed NMR.
  • ​The NMR signal is recorded while the CW excitation is ongoing; in pulsed NMR the signal is recorded after the pulse is over.
Absorption and Dispersion Line Shapes
The ideal smooth NMR signal illustrated below occurs only in the so-called slow-passage regime, where the sweep rate is slow compared to relaxation. In this scenario, the spin system remains in a near steady state while the field changes and relaxation continuously adjusts to the magnetization. Adiabatic following of the magnetization is required, where the direction of the effective magnetic field must change much more slowly than the rate at which the nuclear spins precess. 
CW NMR spectrum of ethyl alcohol. From left to right with decreasing field strength, the three absorption peaks arise from the CH3, CH2, and −OH hydrogens respectively. This is Figure 6 reproduced from Edward Purcell's Nobel Lecture of 1952.
Idealized absorption (red) and dispersion (blue) line shapes of a CW NMR signal performed during a slow-passage experiment.
Depending on the phase of the recorded signal with respect to the RF, the spectral peaks may appear as absorptive (A, in phase), dispersive (D, 90° out of phase), or a mixture of the two. Note that A and D do not come from separate experiments; they are two quadrature components of the same response.​ Absorption corresponds to the dissipative, energy-absorbing response and produces a symmetric Lorentzian line shape, while dispersion corresponds to the reactive, phase-shifted response and produces an antisymmetric line shape. Even in the modern era of pulsed NMR, absorption display of spectra are usually preferred over dispersion displays for the following reasons:
  • Narrower peak shape and width, making it easier to separate closely spaced lines
  • Clearer identification of maximum amplitude corresponding to precise resonance frequency
  • Easier peak area integration for quantification
  • Easier phase correction
CW MR Spectra with Faster Sweeps 
When used to evaluate to complex molecular structures, slow-passage CW NMR experiments rapidly fell out of favor due to their prolonged imaging time and low signal-to-noise. The general practice in the CW NMR era evolved into performing intermediate- or fast-passage sweeps as shown in the historical tracing below.
Paper recording of the CW ¹H NMR spectrum of Ortho-di-chloro-benzene (ODCB). Note the FID-like "wiggles" after each peak.
When the RF-field was swept faster than the spins could adiabatically follow, new transverse magnetization was generated suddenly and began precessing freely after leaving resonance. This resulted in an oscillatory transient after passing through a resonance peak whose envelope decayed with time-constant T2*. Initially called "the wiggles", this oscillation  closely resembled what would later be known as the free induction decay (FID), the only difference being that the oscillation frequency is constant with and FID but increases during the wiggle due to the progressive increased magnetic field occurring with continued sweep.

Applications of CW NMR Today

Although by the 1980s CW NMR had been nearly completely supplanted by Pulsed NMR, Its survives today mainly in education, low-field relaxometry, niche materials studies, and hybrid pulsed NMR experiments for proton decoupling, spin locking, and saturation transfer. However, continuous wave techniques remain prominent in a closely related field, electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR). Here because electron spins have much larger magnetic moments and faster relaxation times than nuclear spins, steady-state absorption is strong, rapid, and information-rich.


Transition to the Steady State

When the CW B1 field is first activated, it produces a torque on M causing the entire spin system to begin a transient nutation in the rotating frame similar to that seen during a short duration RF-pulse. Because the motion is initially coherent, Mz can overshoot the transverse plane and become negative. This is known as "partial inversion during transient nutation." So early in its evolution, Mz may oscillate and cross zero several times. Transient inversion is most likely when ω1 is large, T2 is long (weak damping), and the RF field is turned on suddenly. Under those conditions the initial motion resembles Rabi oscillations.


Why T2 Appears in the Saturation Factor

True NMR saturation requires T1 relaxation because equalizing the Zeeman populations requires energy transfer to the lattice. Pure T2 processes alone cannot produce saturation. However, T2 plays an important role because it governs the lifetime of transverse coherence and therefore the efficiency with which RF irradiation can convert longitudinal magnetization into transverse coherence that is subsequently dissipated via T1.

Analysis of this issue by the density-matrix reveals the causal chain: RF → coherence → population change. RF does not directly equalize populations—it does so through creation of transverse coherence. The amount of coherence that can build up is limited by T2. That coherence drives population transfer. Population recovery occurs through T1.

So, saturation efficiency depends both on T2 (how much coherence can build up) and T1 (how quickly populaitons recover). Multiplying the two gives s = ω²1T1T2.
